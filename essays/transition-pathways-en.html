On the Difficulty of Killing a Regime

Collapse, Transition, and Governance in Post-Islamic Republic Iran

1404/12/08

Executive Summary

This brief provides an operational-level assessment of four questions: whether military strikes can initiate the collapse of the Islamic Republic; what mechanisms could plausibly trigger regime failure; what transition scenarios follow from each collapse pathway; and whether a decentralized governance model without a supreme authority is feasible for post-Islamic Republic Iran. Unlike conventional policy treatments, this analysis begins with a granular map of how the regime actually holds power –because any assessment of collapse or transition that does not account for the Islamic Republic's specific control architecture will reach incorrect conclusions.

Key findings: The Islamic Republic's survival system is not a conventional authoritarian state with a military wing. It is a layered, redundant apparatus in which the Supreme Leader's personal network (the Beit-e Rahbari), the IRGC's parallel military-economic complex, and the Basij's capillary neighborhood surveillance function as interlocking systems –each capable of sustaining regime control even when the others are degraded. Military strikes, including Operation Rising Lion (June 2025), degrade specific nodes but do not sever these interlocking systems. The December 2025–January 2026 massacres –with verified death tolls between 7,007 (HRANA named list) and potentially over 30,000 (Ministry of Health internal estimates) –demonstrate that the regime retains the institutional willingness and operational capacity to kill at industrial scale. Transition planning that underestimates the depth and distributed nature of this control architecture faces a high probability of failure.

I. The Regime's Power Architecture: How the Islamic Republic Actually Holds Power

Any analysis of collapse or transition must begin with an accurate map of the control system. The Islamic Republic operates through five interlocking layers, each with independent survival capacity.

Layer 1 –The Supreme Leader's Personal Network (Beit-e Rahbari). The Office of the Supreme Leader is the most powerful and least visible institution in the Islamic Republic. Its core structure includes approximately 4,000 "commissars" who function as the real policymakers across the state, embedded within an umbrella organization of roughly 40,000 individuals entrenched at every layer of government (per reporting by Fox News citing leaked internal documents and corroborated by The National's February 2026 analysis). These are not advisory positions; they are operational control nodes. Khamenei does not govern through institutions –he governs through trusted individuals placed inside institutions. The Beit appoints or approves Friday prayer leaders in every province (who double as intelligence collectors), controls the Guardian Council's vetting of electoral candidates, oversees the judiciary through the appointment of the chief justice, and directs national media through control of IRIB. The financial dimension is equally critical: Setad (Headquarters for Executing the Order of the Imam), which Khamenei was supposed to dismantle after two years, has been expanded into a conglomerate valued at $95 billion (Reuters investigation) to $200 billion (U.S. officials' estimate per Celebrity Net Worth), operating across insurance, energy, telecoms, and real estate –entirely outside parliamentary oversight. Mojtaba Khamenei, the Supreme Leader's son, has assembled an extensive overseas real estate portfolio, moving capital beyond Iran's borders despite the sanctions regime. Power flows through a personal network that operates alongside and above formal state institutions.

Layer 2 –The IRGC's Parallel Military-Economic Complex. The IRGC is not a military force in any conventional sense. It is a parallel state with its own army, navy, air force, missile command, intelligence service (IRGC-IO, established on Khamenei's initiative in 2009, reportedly as powerful as or more powerful than the civilian MOIS), special operations force (Quds Force), and –critically –an economic empire that makes it the single most powerful economic actor in Iran. Iran's 2025 budget allocated the IRGC nearly twice the resources of the conventional army. But the official budget is a fraction of the IRGC's actual revenue. Khatam al-Anbiya Construction Headquarters monopolizes major infrastructure, energy, and transport projects through non-competitive state contracts. The IRGC controls at least 60 unauthorized ports (per NCRI reporting, corroborated by Gulf International Forum analysis) through which it smuggles fuel, drugs, and luxury goods outside government supervision. Its "dark fleet" of tankers conducts ship-to-ship oil transfers using falsified documentation to evade sanctions –responsible for exporting roughly one-third of Iran's oil to fund the IRGC's own budget. The Telecommunication Company of Iran (TCI) is majority-owned by a consortium linked to Setad and the IRGC, giving the regime direct control over communications infrastructure –the mechanism that enabled the complete internet shutdown on 8 January 2026. Bonyad Mostazafan controls vast agricultural, industrial, and commercial holdings across every major region. These are not paper assets. They are operational enterprises managed by local IRGC-linked commanders who control employment, contracts, and coercive capacity in their provinces. The IRGC's 150,000+ personnel include many who are economically dependent on IRGC-linked enterprises –they are not merely soldiers ordered to defend a regime but stakeholders defending a business model.

Layer 3 –The Basij: Capillary Surveillance and Disposable Violence. The Basij Resistance Force operates through approximately 64,000 bases nationwide, organized to penetrate Iranian society at the neighborhood level. Its structure includes student Basij (universities), worker Basij (factories), tribal Basij (rural areas), and neighborhood Basij (residential surveillance). The regime's "neighborhood-based management" program deploys 30,000 neighborhood development councils tasked with completing neighborhood intelligence databases through door-to-door data collection and reconnaissance patrols. This is not a militia in the conventional sense; it is a distributed surveillance and enforcement network that makes every neighborhood legible to the state. The Basij's operational role during crackdowns is to provide the initial coercive layer –street-level violence, identification of protest leaders, enforcement of curfews –while the IRGC provides the command structure and heavy firepower. The Basij proved effective at localized repression in 2022 and again in the December 2025–January 2026 massacres, but cannot sustain coordinated national operations without IRGC command-and-control. This dependency is a structural vulnerability –but one that only matters if the IRGC command chain is itself disrupted.

Layer 4 –The Dual Intelligence Apparatus. Iran operates two competing intelligence services: the Ministry of Intelligence and Security (MOIS), nominally civilian, and the IRGC Intelligence Organization (IRGC-IO). Both conduct domestic surveillance, counterintelligence, and –increasingly –extraterritorial assassination and kidnapping operations. The IRGC-IO, established on Khamenei's direct initiative, has grown to rival or exceed MOIS in capability. Their competition creates redundancy: even if one is compromised, the other maintains surveillance continuity. Both are responsible for the systematic post-protest judicial crackdown now underway –accelerated indictments, expedited trials, heavy sentences, and documented cases of torture and killing in detention.

Layer 5 –The Artesh: Subordinated, Marginalized, Ambiguous. Iran's conventional military has been systematically subordinated since 1979. It is denied offensive operations, excluded from the regime's political economy, sidelined in budget allocations, and kept deliberately weak relative to the IRGC. The Artesh is the only major institution in the Islamic Republic with no direct economic stake in the regime's survival. This makes it the institution most frequently identified as a potential defector in transition scenarios. But its subordination also means its operational capacity –to project power independently, to secure territory against IRGC resistance, to enforce transitional authority –is limited precisely because the regime designed it to be limited.

What this architecture means for collapse analysis: The Islamic Republic does not have a single point of failure. It has multiple, redundant control systems that share some nodes (Khamenei's appointment authority) but can operate semi-independently. A strike that kills IRGC commanders does not disable the Beit's commissar network. A popular uprising that overwhelms the Basij does not necessarily fracture the IRGC. An intelligence failure in MOIS does not silence IRGC-IO. This redundancy is the core design feature of the regime –learned explicitly from the Shah's 1979 collapse, which occurred because the Shah's power was concentrated in his person and a single security service (SAVAK). The Islamic Republic was engineered to survive the assassination of any individual, the failure of any single institution, and the loss of any single source of revenue.

II. Do Military Strikes Cause Regime Collapse?

The empirical record is extensive and consistent. RAND concluded that limited military strikes do not unseat entrenched authoritarian regimes. Cato Institute's systematic review found that forcible regime change fails more often than it succeeds, and targeted states are more likely to experience civil war afterward. The case record reinforces this: Serbia (1999) –78-day NATO bombing did not remove Milosevic; he fell sixteen months later through election dispute plus military refusal to fire on protesters. Iraq (2003) –full invasion removed Saddam but produced catastrophic state collapse, sectarian civil war, and the rise of ISIS. Libya (2011) –NATO airstrikes facilitated Qaddafi's overthrow but produced a failed state that remains fractured.

Iran after June 2025 fits the pattern. Operation Rising Lion killed Hossein Salami (IRGC commander), Mohammed Bagheri (Armed Forces chief of staff), Amir Ali Hajizadeh (IRGC Aerospace commander), and several nuclear scientists. It destroyed an estimated 50–66% of missile launchers, 15 fighter aircraft, multiple air defense systems, and severely damaged Natanz. By conventional military metrics, this was devastating. The regime did not collapse. It replaced killed commanders, created a new Defense Council to institutionalize command continuity, and appointed Ali Larijani as secretary of the Supreme National Security Council. The IRGC adapted tactically, improving its missile success rate from 8% to 16% over the 12-day war, peaking at 37% in the final days.

The reason is structural: the power architecture described in Section I means strikes degrade specific capabilities but do not sever the interlocking control systems. You can kill an IRGC commander, but the Beit commissar in his headquarters continues operating. You can destroy missile launchers, but the IRGC's economic empire –unauthorized ports, dark fleet, Khatam al-Anbiya contracts –continues generating revenue. You can damage Natanz, but the Basij's 64,000 neighborhood bases continue surveilling the population.

The caveat remains: Strikes do not cause collapse but can catalyze it when they intersect with pre-existing internal fragmentation. The June 2025 strikes humiliated the regime before its own population, demonstrated its inability to defend national sovereignty, and created a window of elite vulnerability that contributed to the conditions for the December 2025 uprising.

III. Collapse Mechanisms: Three Conditions, and the December 2025–January 2026 Test Case

Comparative analysis identifies three conditions that must operate simultaneously for authoritarian regime collapse.

Condition 1: Elite Fracture. The ruling coalition must split. In Iran, this means a visible break within the IRGC's competing factions –described as a "constellation of competing cartels" held together by Khamenei's personal authority. The succession question is the primary fracture line: when Khamenei dies or is incapacitated, the mechanism that resolves inter-factional disputes disappears. Competing succession candidates –including Mojtaba Khamenei (with deep IRGC ties, cultivated partly through the late Ayatollah Mesbah Yazdi's hardline network), Sadiq Larijani, Mohammad Mirbagheri, and the symbolically significant Hassan Khomeini –represent genuinely different visions for the regime's future. The IRGC is not a unitary actor: its 150,000+ personnel harbor competing ambitions, and the question of whether the corps will continue deferring to clerical authority or seek direct rule has no settled answer. The June 2025 leadership decapitation accelerated this uncertainty.

Condition 2: Security-Force Defection or Neutrality. The regime falls when those ordered to shoot refuse to shoot. The December 2025–January 2026 events provide the most important recent test of this condition –and the result indicates continued security-force cohesion under extreme stress. The security forces did not refuse. The mass killings of 8–9 January 2026, documented by Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, were carried out by IRGC and Basij units in a planned and organized manner, reportedly ordered by Khamenei himself with approval from all three branches of government. Security forces positioned on streets and rooftops fired rifles and shotguns at protesters, frequently targeting heads and torsos. The verified death tolls –7,007 named dead (HRANA), with internal Ministry of Health estimates of 30,000+ in the first 48 hours –demonstrate that the regime's coercive apparatus not only remained intact but operated at a scale exceeding any previous crackdown (including the 2019 "Bloody November" with estimates ranging from 300 confirmed deaths (Amnesty International) to 1,500 (opposition sources), and the 2022 Mahsa Amini protests with over 300 confirmed dead). This is the single most important data point for transition analysis: the Islamic Republic demonstrated in January 2026 that it can and will kill thousands of its own citizens, and that its security forces will carry out the orders. Any transition scenario must account for this demonstrated capability and willingness.

Condition 3: Sustained Mass Mobilization. The December 2025 protests were the largest since 1979, initially triggered by inflation, food prices, and currency collapse, but rapidly evolving into explicit demands for the end of the Islamic government. The protests spread from Tehran's Grand Bazaar to Isfahan, Shiraz, Mashhad, Qom, Bandar Abbas, and dozens of smaller cities across multiple provinces. This demonstrated massive, cross-class, cross-ethnic mobilization capacity. But the January 2026 massacres also demonstrated the regime's capacity to suppress that mobilization through industrial-scale violence followed by total internet shutdown (since 8 January 2026) and systematic judicial repression –accelerated indictments, expedited trials, heavy sentences, and documented torture and killing in detention, including reports of women's bodies returned with missing organs.

Assessment: The December 2025–January 2026 crisis demonstrated Condition 3 (mass mobilization) at unprecedented scale, but Conditions 1 and 2 did not materialize. The elite did not fracture visibly –all three branches of government endorsed the crackdown. The security forces did not defect –they killed at orders. The regime survived the largest domestic challenge in its history because two of the three necessary conditions were absent. This is an empirical finding, not a political judgment. It means the most probable path to collapse remains the one that disrupts Conditions 1 and 2 from within: Khamenei's death or incapacitation triggering a succession crisis that fractures the elite, combined with economic deterioration severe enough that security-force personnel's material incentives shift. External shock (strikes) and mass mobilization are accelerants, not ignition sources.

IV. Transition Scenarios: Four Pathways

Based on the collapse mechanisms above, drawing on the Stimson Center's scenario framework and calibrated against Geddes, Wright, and Frantz's N=25 post-authoritarian transitions since 1974.

Pathway A –Negotiated Transition (probability: ~10–15%). Analogous to Spain (1975–78) or South Africa (1990–94). A reformist faction negotiates the regime's departure in exchange for guarantees. Currently the least plausible pathway: the January 2026 massacres have destroyed whatever remaining credibility reformists within the system possessed, and the opposition has no incentive to negotiate with figures complicit in mass killing. If it occurs, it produces the highest institutional continuity and lowest violence.

Pathway B –Popular Revolution with Military Defection (~25–30%). Analogous to Tunisia (2011) or the Philippines (1986). Mass protests trigger Artesh neutrality or defection; IRGC fragments; regime loses coercive capacity. This is the pathway the IPP Emergency Phase plan implicitly assumes. However, the January 2026 evidence makes this less probable than it appeared before the crackdown –the security forces demonstrated willingness to kill at scale without defecting. For this pathway to materialize, something must change in the security-force calculus: either economic collapse so severe that IRGC personnel's material incentives erode (if sanctions cut the dark fleet revenue and Khatam al-Anbiya contracts dry up), or a succession crisis that splits IRGC commanders into competing factions without a single authority to give the order.

Pathway C –Regime Implosion Following Succession Crisis (~30–35%). Elements of the Soviet Union (1991) and Yugoslavia (1991–92). Khamenei's death or incapacitation triggers a succession struggle. The Beit-e Rahbari network loses its central coordinator. Competing IRGC factions back different candidates. No faction achieves dominance. The regime does not fall to external challenge –it collapses from internal incoherence. This remains the single most likely individual pathway (though still below 50%) because it requires only one exogenous trigger (Khamenei's health, and he is 86) rather than requiring coordination of multiple conditions. The key analytical uncertainty is whether the regime's institutional preparations for succession –the Defense Council created after June 2025, the expansion of the Assembly of Experts' role –are sufficient to manage the transition within the system. The evidence is ambiguous: the preparations exist but have never been tested under the combination of military humiliation, economic crisis, and mass popular rejection that characterizes the current moment.

Pathway D –Fragmented Collapse After External Shock (~15–20%). Analogous to Iraq (2003) or Libya (2011). A new round of U.S. or Israeli strikes coincides with internal crisis, producing uncontrolled disintegration. Multiple power centers –IRGC remnants controlling provincial economic fiefdoms, Artesh units, ethnic militias, returning diaspora factions –compete for control. This is the highest-violence, lowest-continuity scenario. The IRGC's distributed economic control (unauthorized ports, provincial Khatam al-Anbiya operations, Basij base networks) means that even regime "collapse" does not eliminate IRGC-linked local power –it fragments it into warlord-like provincial structures.

Probability note: These are structured analytical judgments derived from comparative case frequency, weighted by Iran-specific conditions. The remaining ~5–10% is allocated to outcomes not captured by these pathways. All estimates carry ±10 point uncertainty.

V. Decentralized Governance Without a Supreme Authority: A Realistic Assessment

The question of whether a decentralized model without a supreme authority can govern post-Islamic Republic Iran must be answered against the power architecture described in Section I –not against theoretical models of federalism.

The case for decentralization remains structurally sound: Iran's defining political pathology is the concentration of unaccountable power in a single religio-political authority. Replacing one supreme leader with another reproduces the vulnerability. Iran's ethno-linguistic composition –over 60% non-Persian, including Azerbaijani Turks (~25%), Kurds (~10%), Lurs (~6%), Arabs (~2%), Baluch (~2%), Turkmen (~2%) –means a Persian-dominated central authority faces immediate legitimacy challenges in the periphery. The Atlantic Council has documented disproportionately high unemployment and low services in border provinces. Kurdish political parties (PDKI, Komala, PJAK) already demand autonomy within a federal framework. Spain's asymmetric comunidades autónomas suggest that accommodation may reduce separatist pressure, though causal inference from a single case requires caution.

However, the case against decentralization is stronger than initial assessments acknowledged:

First, the IRGC's distributed economic empire means that "decentralization" in Iran does not mean devolving power to civilian provincial authorities. It means confronting IRGC-linked local power holders who already control provincial economies –the Khatam al-Anbiya project managers, the unauthorized port operators, the Basij base commanders who know every family in their district. Provincial Transition Councils without the capacity to displace these actors would be advisory bodies operating alongside the real power. This is not a theoretical concern –it is the actual distribution of coercive and economic capacity on the ground.

Second, the January 2026 massacres demonstrated that the regime's security forces will kill thousands at orders. Any transitional authority –centralized or decentralized –must have the coercive capacity to prevent IRGC remnants from mounting precisely this kind of organized violence. The Artesh, which is the only plausible provider of this capacity, has been deliberately weakened by four decades of IRGC dominance. Its ability to secure 31 provinces simultaneously against IRGC resistance is genuinely uncertain.

Third, the Basij's 64,000 neighborhood bases represent a distributed infrastructure of regime loyalists who will not simply dissolve when the regime falls. They constitute a ready-made insurgent or spoiler network –armed, trained, locally knowledgeable, and economically dependent on the regime's survival. Dismantling or co-opting this network is a prerequisite for stable governance of any kind.

A revised model –Provincial Transition Councils with acknowledged constraints:

The three-tier architecture (Provincial Transition Councils → Regional Coordination Blocs → National Coordination Council) remains the most robust structural design for the specific problem of governing a large, diverse, fractured state without a single point of failure. But its feasibility depends entirely on conditions that cannot be assumed:

The PTCs function only if the Artesh cooperates –and the Artesh's willingness to cooperate depends on guarantees of institutional survival, pension security, and protection from both prosecution and IRGC retaliation. These guarantees must be established before Day 1 through pre-transition engagement with sympathetic senior officers.

The Regional Coordination Blocs function only if the IRGC's provincial economic infrastructure is either neutralized, co-opted, or managed. Neutralization (asset seizure) risks armed resistance from IRGC-linked personnel who control local employment. Co-optation (offering amnesty and continued management under civilian oversight) risks embedding regime loyalists in the new system. There is no clean option. The least bad approach is conditional co-optation: local IRGC-linked economic actors who cooperate with transitional authority retain operational management under PTC audit; those who resist face asset seizure backed by Artesh enforcement.

The National Coordination Council functions only if a single entity can speak for Iran internationally –signing oil contracts, negotiating frozen asset release, engaging with the IAEA on the nuclear file. The NCC's supermajority design (10 of 14 votes) and rotating chair sacrifice speed for resilience against capture. This is a genuine trade-off, and the international community's strong preference for a single interlocutor is a real constraint. The mitigation is a permanent NCC Foreign Affairs Spokesperson with sole negotiation authority –one voice abroad, many voices at home.

The Basij problem has no clean solution. A network of 64,000 bases with armed, locally embedded personnel cannot be dismantled overnight without creating mass unemployment and a ready-made insurgency. It cannot be absorbed into the new security structures without embedding regime loyalists. The most plausible approach is geographic triage: in provinces where the Basij is weakest (typically the periphery, where ethnic and tribal structures provide alternative social organization), dismantle and disarm. In provinces where the Basij is strongest (Tehran, Isfahan, Mashhad, Qom), offer individual amnesty conditional on disarmament and registration, while treating organized resistance as a security threat subject to Artesh enforcement.

VI. Failure Conditions

This architecture fails under the following conditions, each of which is plausible:

It fails if the Artesh does not cooperate –because no civilian transitional structure has independent coercive capacity against IRGC remnants. Countermeasure: Pre-transition engagement with senior Artesh officers, guaranteeing institutional continuity, pension security, and a defined constitutional role in the post-transition order.

It fails if IRGC economic stakeholders mount organized armed resistance to asset restructuring –transforming a political transition into a civil war. Countermeasure: Conditional co-optation (amnesty-for-cooperation) for mid-level IRGC economic actors; targeted international sanctions maintained against senior commanders who resist, backed by multilateral non-interference commitments from neighbors.

It fails if Khamenei's succession is managed successfully within the system –producing a new Supreme Leader with sufficient IRGC backing to maintain control. This is the scenario in which no transition occurs. Assessment: Possible but increasingly difficult given the simultaneous pressures of military humiliation, economic crisis, and mass popular rejection. The regime's preparations for succession exist but have never been tested under these conditions.

It fails if economic collapse is so severe that provincial governance cannot sustain essential services. Provincial autonomy without revenue is meaningless. Countermeasure: Pre-negotiated emergency credit lines from IMF or bilateral donors, conditioned on governance transparency; immediate resumption of oil exports, requiring only that the NCC can sign contracts and receive payment.

It fails if Iran's neighbors exploit provincial autonomy to project influence through ethnic kinship networks –Turkey with Azerbaijanis, Pakistan with Baluch, Iraq with Arab Khuzestan. Countermeasure: Immediate multilateral non-interference agreements backed by UN or EU monitoring, with reciprocal commitments.

VII. What We Do Not Know and Cannot Control

There is a category difference between things this analysis chose not to cover and things no analysis can cover. Both matter. The second category matters more, because it is where plans go to die.

Things we do not know but someone might: The ethnic composition of the IRGC and Basij –whether units are drawn from specific groups or are nationally representative –would reshape every fracture scenario in this document. If the security forces are ethnically skewed, succession crises split along ethnic lines, not just factional ones. The data does not exist in open sources. It may exist in classified intelligence holdings. Anyone planning a transition without it is guessing at the fault lines.

The women's movement –which ignited the 2022 uprising and was central to December 2025 –appears in this analysis only as a contributor to mass mobilization. It is not modeled as a political actor in transition architecture. This is a gap, not a judgment. Any operational plan that treats half the population as a mobilization input rather than a governance actor has a design flaw.

The diaspora (4–5 million, mostly in the U.S., Europe, and Turkey) is a variable that cuts both ways with no way to predict which way it cuts. Diaspora factions carry money, competing political visions, and relationships with foreign powers. They could stabilize a transition or tear it apart. The determining factor –which factions gain early access to transitional institutions –is a function of timing and opportunity, not planning.

Things no one knows and no model can predict: The nuclear program imposes constraints on any transitional authority that are real but whose shape is unknowable in advance. IAEA inspections, NPT obligations, and sanctions conditionality will limit sovereignty during transition –but exactly how depends on the geopolitical moment of collapse, which no one controls. A transition during a U.S. election year faces different nuclear constraints than one during a Middle East escalation. The interaction between these variables is not complicated –it is complex, meaning it cannot be modeled, only navigated in real time.

The sanctions relief timeline belongs in this category. Every precedent (Iraq, Libya) shows that sanctions and asset freezes persist months or years after regime change. The transitional authority will be revenue-starved regardless of what diplomats promise beforehand. The gap between "sanctions will be lifted" and "sanctions are actually lifted" has bankrupted transitional governments before. No pre-negotiated agreement eliminates this risk –it can only shorten it.

The most consequential unknown is the simplest: when. Every probability estimate in this document is conditional on a timeframe it cannot specify. The regime could face a succession crisis next month or in ten years. The economic, military, and social conditions at the moment of crisis determine which pathway activates –and those conditions are not static. An analysis written in February 2026 describes a snapshot. The regime and its opponents are both moving targets.

The operational implication is asymmetric. The cost of overestimating what we know is catastrophic –it produces rigid plans that shatter on contact with reality. The cost of underestimating what we know is merely inefficient –it produces flexible frameworks that adapt slower than they could. Any honest risk assessment chooses the second error over the first.

Assessment

The Islamic Republic's power architecture is deeper, more distributed, and more redundant than most transition plans acknowledge. The January 2026 massacres demonstrated that this architecture retains both the institutional willingness and operational capacity to kill at industrial scale. Any plan that assumes the regime will crumble under popular pressure alone –without addressing the IRGC's economic empire, the Beit's commissar network, the Basij's capillary surveillance, and the dual intelligence apparatus –is planning for a scenario that the evidence does not support.

Collapse remains plausible, most likely through Pathway C (succession crisis), but the timing is uncertain and the outcome under any pathway will be messier, bloodier, and more contested than optimistic transition blueprints suggest. A decentralized governance model without a supreme authority is structurally superior to centralized alternatives for Iran's specific conditions –but its feasibility depends on solving the IRGC economic displacement problem, securing Artesh cooperation, and managing the Basij dismantlement, none of which have clean solutions.

The conclusion supported by transition evidence is that Iran's transition, when it comes, will not resemble any plan written in advance. The value of planning is not prediction but preparation –building the institutional frameworks, pre-establishing the relationships, and identifying the decision points that allow actors on the ground to adapt to conditions no analyst can fully anticipate.
