A Woman in Zahedan Has No Water

And someone is talking about territorial integrity

1404/12/14

1. The smell of rot

Fifty years. Fifty years of a nation's life have passed, and Sistan-Baluchestan still has no drinking water. Khuzestan, the same Khuzestan under whose soil eighty percent of this country's oil lies sleeping, smells of sewage in summer. Its wetlands have dried up. Its young are jobless. Not ordinary jobless: double the national average.

Kurdistan, Kermanshah, Ilam: no proper roads, no investment, no budget you could do anything with. Turkmen Sahra does not exist economically. The photograph of Iran at night from space has its beautiful points of light: Tehran, Isfahan, Shiraz, Mashhad, and a few others. The rest are asleep. Dark. Not a poetic darkness. A real darkness. The darkness of absence.

This is not an accident. The Islamic Republic is a suction machine (and I would love for all these verbs to become past tense soon): it produces the periphery, the center consumes. The Guard, which holds between twenty and forty percent of the economy, circulates capital through networks where political loyalty is the price of entry. The people of the margins do not get less because they are forgotten. They get less because the system was designed this way.

So when someone in Ahvaz or Zahedan or Mahabad hears that "territorial integrity must be preserved," they have a right to ask: whose integrity? For whose benefit? The status quo is not neutral. The status quo is an organized redistribution from the periphery to the center, held in place by force. Security forces are stationed in these regions not because the separatists are strong, but because the grievances are legitimate. The regime knows this itself.

2. Now let us do the math

So, the grievances are real. The injustice is documented. But does the prescription of "independence" follow from that diagnosis? This is where we have to take off the glasses of emotion and look with open eyes.

What does an independent Baluchestan look like? The same arid geography, the same underdeveloped infrastructure, only now without even the small sums that used to flow from Tehran. On one side, Pakistan, which has its own restive Baluch and sees any independent Baluch state as an existential threat. On the other, a severed and furious Iran, with every incentive to destabilize its new neighbor.

An independent Khuzestan has oil, yes. But the refineries, the pipelines, the export infrastructure all run through the rest of Iran. You cannot draw a line on a map and say this revenue is ours. Oil revenue without infrastructure is just a number on paper.

An independent Kurdistan? Turkey and Iraq enter the game at once. Turkey treats any independent Kurdish state as its red line. Iraq has its own Kurdistan, and its balance comes undone. A separate Azerbaijan? The question of union with the Republic of Azerbaijan arises, and Russia grows angry.

There is a historical example the partition enthusiasts do not like to talk about: South Sudan. It was exactly this story. An oil-rich periphery, an exploiting center, years of struggle, finally independence. The result? Two years after independence, civil war broke out. Famine came. Ethnic cleansing between the Dinka and the Nuer. Oil revenue, instead of reaching the people who had been oppressed by Khartoum, went into the pockets of warlords. The logic of exploitation did not disappear. Only its flag changed. And we Iranians have learned, bitterly, in flesh and bone, what it means to change a flag.

And one more thing no one likes to say aloud: the nuclear question. An Iran that is breaking apart has enrichment facilities. It has missiles. It has technical knowledge. It has the best universities in the Middle East. When the central government collapses, who is sitting on top of these things? Several states or several armed territories with access to fissile material: this is the worst scenario, one that no non-proliferation framework can answer.

3. So what is the answer?

So, partition is not the answer. But "territorial integrity" is not in itself an answer either. It is just a slogan, behind which fifty years of structural injustice and murder and rape and unmarked graves have been hidden. So what can actually be done? Let us look at a few models that have been tried elsewhere in the world. Not with optimism, but with open eyes.

Real fiscal federalism. This means the provinces keep a defined share of their local revenue and have authority over their own education, health, infrastructure, and police. The center keeps only defense, foreign policy, and monetary policy. Switzerland did this: twenty-six cantons, four languages, and one of the most stable countries in the world. But Switzerland had centuries. Iran does not.

A closer example is Spain after Franco. The central government devolved power to autonomous regions, and here is the key point: it did so asymmetrically. The Basque Country got fiscal powers that Andalusia did not. Catalonia got privileges that Valencia did not. Why? Because their problems were not the same. The result: ETA dissolved, Basque separatism subsided. It is not perfect. Catalonia is still contentious. But the framework prevented partition.

Resource revenue sharing. Khuzestan produces eighty percent of Iran's oil and receives a fraction of the development budget. This is indefensible by any standard. If the constitution guaranteed that, say, twenty to twenty-five percent of a province's oil revenue stayed in the province itself, managed by a locally elected body, not a governor appointed by Tehran, Khuzestan's economic equation would change at once. Nigeria did this. Imperfectly, with corruption, but it established the principle that "the producing region has priority over extraction revenue."

Asymmetric autonomy. This may be the most realistic model. The Kurdish problem, the problem of the Arabs of Khuzestan, the Baluch problem are not the same. The Kurds want political and cultural autonomy: language in schools, media in Kurdish, a local assembly with real power. The Arabs of Khuzestan want economic justice and environmental restoration. The Baluch want basic development and an end to security repression. The Azeris want cultural recognition, not separation. A single one-size prescription of "federalism" gives everyone either too much or too little.

But, and this is the big but, all three models have one precondition: a change of regime. The Islamic Republic does not federalize. Its power is built on centralization. The Supreme Leader, the Guardian Council, the economic empire of the Guard all feed on centralization. Federalism demands not the reform of this system but its replacement. Make no mistake: with these scoundrels we will get nowhere.

And this is where the most important investment becomes clear: building organized, competent, non-sectarian political institutions among the people of Iran's periphery. Not to fight, but to be ready when the chance to govern arrives. Iraqi Kurdistan was relatively successful because the Democratic Party and the Patriotic Union had a decade of governing experience before the 2003 constitution formalized their autonomy. Without this readiness, any transition becomes Libya: the dictator goes, and no one knows how to run anything.

And what does not work? Let me be blunt. Imposing partition from outside has never produced stability. Not in India and Pakistan, not in Palestine, not in Iraq after 2003. "Regime change and everything fixes itself" is also an illusion. The Pahlavi state was centralized and Persian-centric too. Without structural decentralization, the new system is the same old suction machine with better public relations.

4. When the voices go silent

The least harmful path, the one with the most empirical support from comparable cases, is asymmetric federalism within the framework of a rebuilt Iran. With revenue-sharing formulas guaranteed in the constitution. With cultural and linguistic autonomy. With locally elected governors who hold real fiscal authority. With asymmetric arrangements that accept that the Kurdish problem, the Khuzestani Arab problem, and the Baluch problem are three different questions and need three different answers.

It is not exciting. It is not revolutionary. It makes for no good propaganda for any faction. It demands the hardest political work imaginable: institution-building, negotiation, accepting imperfect outcomes.

But.

What is the alternative? A partition that spills the blood of the very people whose rights were trampled? A status quo that has been tested for fifty years and failed? Anything else is either fantasy or someone else's war on Iranian soil.

The Khuzestani farmer still has no water tonight. The Baluch woman still has no access to a hospital tonight. The Kurdish youth still does not study in his mother tongue tonight.

And tomorrow it will be the same.

Unless someone, not from outside, from within, builds a structure worthy of them. And that has not happened yet.

Tomorrow the sun rises again. Tomorrow, again, there is no one.
