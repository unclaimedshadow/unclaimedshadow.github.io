Israel, Seen Clearly

1405/03/16

A way in: a tomb in Hamadan

In the middle of Hamadan, an old Iranian city, there is a small brick shrine with a domed roof. Inside are two graves. For centuries, Iranian Jews have believed they belong to Esther and Mordechai, the queen and the courtier from the Book of Esther, a story set entirely in the Persian court. Whether the bones are really theirs, we honestly do not know. The building is medieval, not ancient, and historians argue about it. But the shrine is real, it has stood in Iran for a very long time, and for generations Iranian Muslims and Jews both treated it as holy ground.

Start here, because it tells you something that fifty years of state television has tried to make you forget. Jews are not strangers to Iran. They are one of the oldest living threads in Iranian history, older than Islam, older than the Persian language as we speak it now. To understand Israel honestly, an Iranian has to begin by un-learning the idea that "Jew," "Israel," and "enemy" are the same word. They are not. This essay is an attempt to look at Israel the way you would look at any other country: as a place with a history, a population, real achievements, real crimes, and real human beings, none of which fit on a propaganda poster.

A warning before we start. The goal here is not to make you love Israel or hate it. The goal is to let you see it. Governments, both in Tehran and in Jerusalem, have strong reasons to hand you a cartoon. A cartoon is easy to cheer for and easy to hate. A real country is harder, and more interesting.

What are we even looking at?

Israel is a small country on the eastern shore of the Mediterranean Sea, about the size of the Iranian province of Hormozgan. As of the start of 2026 it had roughly 10.2 million people. That is smaller than Tehran's metropolitan area. It declared independence on 14 May 1948, which makes it younger than many Iranians' grandparents.

So when people speak as if Israel were an ancient, eternal enemy of Iran, the calendar alone should make you pause. For all but the last 77 years of the roughly 2,700 years that Jews and Iranians have known each other, there was no State of Israel to be an enemy of. The hostility is recent. The relationship is ancient. Keep those two facts apart in your mind and most of the fog clears.

How the country came to exist

The modern story begins not in the Middle East but in Europe, in the late 1800s. Jews across Europe lived as a permanent minority, often tolerated, periodically slaughtered. In the Russian Empire, mobs carried out organized massacres called pogroms. In supposedly civilized France, a Jewish army officer named Dreyfus was framed for treason while crowds shouted "death to the Jews." Out of this fear grew a movement called Zionism: the idea that Jews would never be safe as a minority in other people's countries, and needed a country of their own. An Austro-Hungarian journalist, Theodor Herzl, became its most famous voice. Zionists looked to the land they called by its biblical name, then a province of the Ottoman Empire and home to a mostly Arab population.

This is the first place where an honest account has to slow down. The land was not empty. Arabs, Muslim and Christian, had lived there for centuries and were the large majority. Two peoples came to want the same small piece of ground, and both had reasons that felt absolute to them. That collision is the root of everything that followed, and no fair telling can pretend one side simply appeared from nowhere onto empty soil.

Then came the catastrophe that changed everything. Between 1941 and 1945, Nazi Germany and its collaborators murdered about six million Jews, two-thirds of all the Jews in Europe, in an industrial program of extermination we call the Holocaust. This is not propaganda and it is not exaggeration; it is one of the most thoroughly documented events in human history, recorded in the murderers' own files. For the survivors, the lesson was brutal and simple: the world had watched, and the world had not saved them. The demand for a Jewish state stopped being a debate and became, for many, a matter of survival.

In 1947 the United Nations voted to divide British-ruled Palestine into two states, one Jewish and one Arab. The Jewish leadership accepted; the Arab leadership and the surrounding Arab states rejected it. When Israel declared independence in May 1948, five Arab armies invaded. Israel survived the war. In the same war, more than 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were driven from their homes, an event Palestinians call the Nakba, the catastrophe. Both of these things are true at once. For Israelis, 1948 is independence and rescue. For Palestinians, it is dispossession and loss. A reader who can hold both of those without flinching understands the conflict better than most governments pretend to.

Here the oldest rule of reading history applies: doubt your own yardstick. It is tempting for an Iranian raised on Islamic regime slogans to judge Israel's birth as uniquely sinful. But partition, expulsion, and borders drawn across other people's homes are how almost every modern nation-state was made, and that includes Iran. Look at a map of the region and the cuts are everywhere. The Sasanian kings of Iran once ruled from Ctesiphon, and its giant brick arch, the Taq-e Kasra (Tagh-e Kasra, "Khosrow's vault"), still stands beside the Tigris inside Iraq. Samarkand and Bukhara, two jewels of Persian poetry and the Persian-speaking world, sit today in Uzbekistan, while next door Tajikistan speaks Persian but was fenced into its own Soviet republic. And when a defeated Iran signed two treaties with Russia, in 1813 and again in 1828, it lost the entire South Caucasus and was handed a new border, the Aras river, drawn straight through the Azerbaijani people, leaving families to wave at cousins on the far bank. After the First World War, France and Britain did the same thing on a grand scale, ruling lines across the Middle East with little care for who lived inside them. If those Iranian wounds feel faint while Israel's feels raw, that gap is worth sitting with, because the reason is not that ours were gentle. It is distance and memory. The families split by the Aras have been gone for a century, and Iran folded their loss into a single bitter word, Turkmenchay, a byword for national humiliation, while letting the divided brothers and sisters themselves slip out of the story. Israel's birth is younger, still inside living memory, and both sides retell it out loud every year, so we feel its ache up close: not because it is uniquely large, but because it is near and constantly narrated. An Iranian, of all readers, lives inside a map carved by the very forces that carved Israel. None of this makes dispossession clean. It means Israel's founding was neither a pure miracle nor a unique crime. It was a hard, contested, very twentieth-century event, with victims on more than one side.

The people: who actually lives there

Strip away the flags and look at the faces. Israel's roughly 10.2 million people break down, in round 2025 numbers, into about 7.7 million Jews and "others" (around 76 percent), about 2.1 million Arabs (around 21 percent), and a few hundred thousand others. The Arab citizens of Israel are not a footnote. They vote, sit in parliament, serve on the supreme court, run hospitals. Their situation is genuinely contested, with real complaints of discrimination, and an Iranian should not airbrush that. But the simple existence of two million Arab citizens already breaks the cartoon of Israel as a place that wants no Arabs in it.

Now turn that same test, honestly, on Iran. Iran does reserve a handful of parliament seats for its recognized minorities, the Jews, Christians, and Zoroastrians, and that is real and worth knowing. But the top of the state is sealed: the constitution keeps the presidency for a Shia Muslim, so a Jew, a Christian, a Sunni, or a Baha'i cannot hold it however the country votes. The Baha'is, the largest of the unrecognized faiths, are not merely shut out but hunted, barred from universities and jobs and sometimes jailed or executed for their belief. And many minorities did not stay to be counted: Iran's Jewish community, one of the oldest on earth, has fallen from perhaps eighty thousand before 1979 to a fraction of that, scattered abroad.

Or take the Armenians, woven into Iran for four centuries, ever since Shah Abbas resettled them from the Aras valley into the Isfahan quarter they made famous, New Julfa. They gave Iran far more than their numbers: the merchants who ran its silk trade, the hands that raised its churches and bridges, the commander Yeprem Khan who helped the Constitutional Revolution take Tehran in 1909, Ovanes Ohanian who directed the first Iranian feature film, and Vigen Derderian, an Armenian boy who grew up to be crowned the king of Iranian pop. For all that loyalty, look where their grandchildren are now. Fly to Glendale, California, across town from wealthy Persian Westwood, and order your coffee in Persian; you will be answered, fluently, by Iranians who carried their language and their love of the place out the door when Iran stopped feeling like home. An Iranian who likes to count Arab faces in Israel's parliament should be willing to count the empty chairs in Tehran too.

Now the part most surprising to an Iranian audience. A very large share of Israeli Jews are not pale Europeans at all. They are Mizrahi Jews, "Eastern" Jews, whose families came from Iraq, Yemen, Morocco, and yes, from Iran. After 1948, the old Jewish communities across the Muslim world were squeezed out, by fear, by riots, and by their own governments, and most of them ended up in Israel. So when an Iranian imagines "the Israelis," a more accurate picture includes people whose grandmothers cooked the same stews, spoke dialects of the same languages, and sang in the same scales as their Muslim neighbors. There are tens of thousands of Israelis today who still speak Persian at home and follow Iranian news more closely than most Iranians abroad. The Israeli army's spokesman for years addressed Iran in fluent Persian on social media, because he grew up speaking it.

This is worth sitting with. The wall between "us" and "them" that the Islamic Republic insists on is not a wall between nations. It is a wall between governments. On the human level, Iran and Israel are stitched together by family, food, language, and a shared 2,700-year story.

Iran and Israel: enemies for a moment, friends for a much longer one

Here is the history that the Islamic regime works hardest to hide, because it is true and it is recent enough to check.

Go back to the start of the Jewish-Persian relationship and the record is not one of war. In 539 BCE Cyrus the Great (Kourosh) took Babylon, where a community of Judeans had been held in exile since the Babylonians sacked Jerusalem decades before. According to the Hebrew Bible, Cyrus let them return home and rebuild their temple; how literally that played out is debated by historians, but the reverence is not. The Book of Isaiah calls Cyrus God's "anointed" (in Hebrew mashiach, the root of "messiah"), a title otherwise kept for kings of Israel and given to no other foreigner anywhere in the Bible. A Persian conqueror sits in Jewish scripture as a deliverer, which is a stubborn fact to set beside a half-century of being told that Jew and Iranian are natural enemies. One correction belongs here, because it attaches to this story constantly: the clay object called the Cyrus Cylinder is not "the first charter of human rights." It is a conventional Babylonian royal inscription that credits the god Marduk and describes restoring local cults; it never mentions Jews, Jerusalem, or rights, and the human-rights reading rests on a mistranslation popularized in the twentieth century. The Cylinder and the biblical return are two separate things that later admirers fused. What survives scrutiny is narrower but real: Persian rule, as the Judeans remembered it, began with a return rather than a deportation.

That long friendliness was not just ancient. Jump to the twentieth century. When Israel was founded in 1948, Iran under the Shah became the second Muslim-majority country in the world to recognize it. From the 1950s until 1979, Iran and Israel were quiet but close partners. Iran sold Israel a large share of its oil, at one point most of it, through a discreet pipeline. The national airlines flew between Tehran and Tel Aviv. Israeli engineers helped build up Iranian farms; Iranian students studied at Israeli universities. Both governments feared the same things: Arab nationalism led by Egypt's Nasser, and Soviet pressure from the north. Israel's intelligence service even helped train the Shah's secret police. None of this is hidden; it is in the standard reference works, including Encyclopaedia Iranica's own article on Israel-Iran relations.

So the enmity between the two states is not 2,700 years old. It is not even 100 years old. It dates precisely to 1979.

When Ayatollah Khomeini's revolution overthrew the Shah, it did not just change Iran's government; it rewrote Iran's enemies. Within days, the new government cut all ties with Israel, handed the Israeli embassy building in Tehran to the Palestine Liberation Organization, and made hatred of Israel a pillar of the state. This was a choice, not a destiny. Notice the contingency here, because it matters: nothing in Iranian history, geography, or religion forced Iran and Israel to become enemies. A revolution made an ideological decision, and that decision has cost both peoples dearly ever since.

For decades the two countries fought through proxies and shadows: Israel against groups Iran armed in Lebanon and Gaza, Iran against Israeli targets through allies and, Israel says, through assassinations and sabotage. Then, in the 2020s, the shadow war stepped into the open. In April and October of 2024, Iran and Israel struck each other's territory directly for the first time. In June 2025 it became a real war.

What they have built: Israel as a center of technology

Set the politics aside and ask a question the Islamic regime never wants asked: what has this small country actually made? A great deal, far out of proportion to its size, and much of it in the very fields where Iran is now failing hardest. An Iranian, heir to a civilization that prized learning, can see that plainly without endorsing any government.

Water, the clearest case. Israel sits in a desert and was short of water for most of its history, then engineered its way to a surplus. Simcha Blass turned a leaking pipe and a thriving tree into modern drip irrigation, water fed drop by drop to the roots, wasting almost nothing; the firm he helped start, Netafim, now sells it worldwide. Israel went on to build desalination plants that turn seawater into drinking water, to recycle the large majority of its wastewater back onto its farms, and to export both the water and the technology. Iran is sliding into a water collapse of its own: aquifers drained, wells run dry, the ground sinking under whole cities. The country with the best answers to that problem is the one its government calls the enemy.

Medicine, farming, and science. Israeli researchers built the PillCam, a swallowable camera that photographs the gut without surgery, and do front-rank work in genetics, cancer drugs, and dryland farming, coaxing crops from land much like Iran's own parched plateau. For its size the country has gathered a remarkable haul of Nobel Prizes, among them the chemist Ada Yonath, who mapped part of the ribosome, the cell's protein factory, and the psychologist Daniel Kahneman, whose study of how human judgment fails is read all over the world.

Computers and everyday tech. Early USB flash drives, the navigation app Waze, and Mobileye, a leader in the software that lets cars see the road, were all developed in Israel. Major American firms run their biggest research centers outside the United States there, and the nickname "Startup Nation" is mostly earned.

Not all of it is benign, and honesty requires saying so. Israel is a major arms maker, its Iron Dome famous from the wars above, and Israeli firms have built phone-hacking and surveillance tools sold to other governments and turned against journalists and dissidents. Technology is a tool; the same country that solved its water problem also builds weapons and spyware. Both belong in the picture.

Then the point sharpens. Iran's deepest problems, water, dry farms, medicine, are Israel's specialties, and before 1979 the two countries already worked on exactly these things together, Israeli engineers in Iranian fields, Iranian students in Israeli labs. An Iran without the present quarrel would not be reaching for a sentimental friend; it would be reaching for the country whose strengths happen to fit its needs most closely. That is not a wish. It is a match of problems to solutions, held apart for now by politics alone.

What we don't know, and what is genuinely contested

A clear mind is not just a mind full of facts; it is a mind honest about the edges of what it knows. So here are the real uncertainties and the real arguments, laid out without a tidy verdict.

The nuclear question. It is widely believed by essentially all serious analysts that Israel possesses nuclear weapons, probably around ninety warheads, built quietly from the 1960s around a reactor at Dimona. Yet Israel has never confirmed or denied this. It calls the policy "amimut," deliberate ambiguity, and it never signed the international Non-Proliferation Treaty. So we are in the strange position of being fairly sure a thing exists while its owner refuses to say. An Iranian should notice the double standard this creates in world politics, and also notice that the standard is real and worth arguing about, not just a slogan.

But hold one more thing in view, or you risk trading one distortion for another. Noticing the double standard is one thing; being the right party to protest it is another, and the Islamic Republic is not that party. A government with its hands deep in corruption, in terrorism beyond its borders, and in the blood of its own people has no standing to lecture the world about anyone's weapons; its grievance can be factually true and still come from the wrong mouth. Picture the same case made by a legitimate, accountable Iranian state, the kind Iran had before 1979, and it would carry real weight and get a fair hearing. And in fairness one should add that such a government would very likely have chosen a wiser course on the nuclear question itself, measured by what actually serves Iran and Iranians, rather than the ruinous standoff that has drained the country and brought war to its skies.

The Palestinians. This is the open wound, and it would be dishonest to bury it under a list of Israeli achievements. Millions of Palestinians live without a state of their own, some as citizens inside Israel, many under military occupation in the West Bank, and others in Gaza, which has seen repeated and devastating wars. Serious people, including many Israelis, argue fiercely about whether this is a security necessity, a temporary tragedy, or a deep injustice that corrodes Israel itself. There is no honest version of Israel that leaves the Palestinians out, just as there is no honest version that reduces Israel to nothing but their suffering. Both the achievement and the wound are real.

There is also a harder, more impatient view that a great many Iranians now say out loud, and an honest account cannot leave it out. After more than forty years of the Islamic Republic pouring money, weapons, and slogans into the Palestinian cause, from the PLO to Hamas and Islamic Jihad, many Iranians feel they have been handed nothing in return but a poorer and more isolated country. They remember that Yasser Arafat and the Palestinian leadership embraced Saddam Hussein, the dictator who invaded Iran in 1980 and dragged it through eight years of war. The street chant "Neither Gaza nor Lebanon, my life for Iran" is the distilled form of this feeling: that a government has no business bankrolling other people's wars while its own citizens go without water and work. One need not share the coldest version of it, "to hell with the Palestinians," to see why it has spread. It is less a verdict on Palestinian suffering, which is real, than a verdict on a regime that spent Iran's wealth on every cause but Iran's.

How Israelis themselves disagree. Perhaps the most useful thing for an Iranian to know is that Israel is a loud, divided democracy. Israelis argue with their own government constantly, fill the streets in protest, and split bitterly over religion, the occupation, the courts, and the wars. There is no single "Israeli view," any more than there is a single "Iranian view." A country you can only see as one angry block is a country you are not really seeing.

How it could have gone otherwise, and why it still echoes

Stand back and the contingency is everywhere. Cyrus could have kept the Jews in exile, and the whole 2,700-year bond might never have formed. The 1947 partition could have been accepted by both sides, and a different, less bloody century might have followed. The 1979 revolution could have kept Iran's quiet partnership with Israel, or simply stayed neutral, and the two peoples might today be trading and traveling instead of trading missiles. None of what happened was written in the stars. People chose, and other choices were available. That is the most hopeful fact in this whole essay, because it means the present arrangement is also a choice, and choices can change.

So what should an Iranian take from all this? Not a new loyalty. Not a swap of one team's flag for another's. The aim was never to turn an enemy into a hero; that would just be propaganda running the other way. The aim is to dissolve the cartoon entirely.

Israel is a small, young country built by traumatized refugees on contested land. It has done genuine harm to the Palestinians and genuine good in science, medicine, and farming. Its people include the descendants of Iranian Jews who still dream in Persian. Its quarrel with Iran is 47 years old, the product of one revolution's choice, not 2,700 years of fate, and for most of the long history before that, Persians and Jews were closer to friends than to foes. The first thing a Persian king is remembered for, in the holiest book of the Jewish people, is setting them free.

The mullahs taught a generation that hating Israel was a form of being a good Iranian. It is worth asking, quietly and for yourself, who benefits from that lesson, and whether a mind that can hold a whole country, its glories and its crimes and its ordinary frightened human beings, all at once, is not a freer and more Iranian mind than one trained to chant at a cartoon.

You do not have to reach any particular conclusion. You only have to look with your own eyes. That, in the end, is the entire point.

Final notes

Recently, Israel and the United States have confronted the Islamic Republic in two major rounds of conflict. In both, one point was striking: there was no visible national rallying around Tehran. Reliable polling inside Iran is difficult, but the pattern is clear enough to note cautiously: many Iranians distinguish sharply between Iran and the Islamic Republic, and some openly see Israel's struggle against Tehran's rulers as aligned with their own desire to end the regime.

That overlap should be stated plainly, but not romanticized. It is not friendship between peoples, nor does it erase the cost of war: governments choose wars, while ordinary people pay for them. Still, the central divide today is less between Iran and Israel than between Iranians and a regime that claims to speak for them.
