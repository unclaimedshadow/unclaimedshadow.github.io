The Inner Court

A play in three acts

1404/12/15

Characters

  • JUDGE
  • PROSECUTOR
  • DEFENSE COUNSEL
  • ME — plaintiff, witness, hidden defendant
  • JUROR 1
  • JUROR 2
  • JUROR 3
  • JUROR 4
  • JUROR 5
  • JUROR 6
  • NARRATOR 1
  • NARRATOR 2
  • SPECTATOR 1 — a man from the south of the city
  • SPECTATOR 2 — a man from the same neighborhood

The defendants, the Father and the Mother, are absent. Two empty chairs at center stage mark their presence.

The stage

(Build the space precisely: the judge's bench upstage, but not quite at the center, slightly to the left. Two empty chairs at center, under a separate light that never goes fully dark. The tables of the prosecutor and defense counsel to either side. The jurors' chairs in shadow: six people you sometimes forget are there until they speak. A lone chair for "Me," facing the two empty chairs. A small balcony for the two narrators, both in clothing that belongs to no period.)

(In the back row, behind the jury, two men are sitting. No formal dress. No suits. Work clothes. No one has paid them any attention. They, too, have no regard for the formalities.)

(The two empty chairs must be in the audience's view the whole length of the play. Even when the light is not on them.)

Act One: The Summons

(The curtain rises. Darkness. Then a sound, not music, not dialogue, only the sound of a chair dragged across the floor. The light comes. Narrator 1 standing. Narrator 2 seated. Both facing the audience.)

NARRATOR 1: This court convenes every night. Not here. Here it is only once. But in the mind of the one who sits there...

(gesturing to the chair of "Me")

...every night. Even across the water.

NARRATOR 2 (without lifting his gaze from some indefinite point): Worse than every night. Every night with a verdict known in advance.

NARRATOR 1: Then why again?

NARRATOR 2: Because a man who knows but cannot help asking... (a pause) ...that is what it is to be a man.

NARRATOR 1 (to the audience, like someone beginning an old tale): There was a son. He had a father and a mother. He had a home. There was a spread on the floor, there was tea, there was talk.

NARRATOR 2: There was.

NARRATOR 1: And one night... no. (a pause) Not one night. A thousand nights. A thousand small moments. (a pause) A cassette tape that disappeared. A sentence cut off in the middle. A silence that sat in the place of an answer. (a pause) Slowly. Like water seeping under a door.

NARRATOR 2: One night?

NARRATOR 1: You know better.

NARRATOR 2: A thousand nights. (a pause) Behind a thousand spreads. In a thousand homes.

(The light comes onto the two empty chairs. Both narrators look at them.)

NARRATOR 1 (more quietly): They did not come.

NARRATOR 2: They do not come.

NARRATOR 1: Maybe they have the right.

NARRATOR 2: Right has nothing to do with coming or not coming.

(The Judge enters. Without ceremony. As if he has come to finish something begun long ago. He sits. Opens a paper, looks at it, sets it down without reading.)

JUDGE: The court is in session.

(A pause. Everyone sits. "Me" enters last. He looks at the two empty chairs, not for long, only a moment, then sits.)

The defendants have not come.

(This sentence is an announcement, not surprise.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (does not rise, only his voice): With permission. Can we proceed without the defendants present?

JUDGE: The defendants were summoned. They did not come. The court does not wait.

DEFENSE COUNSEL: My submission is that a trial in absentia...

JUDGE (without raising his voice, but firm): Counsel. This court is not of the kind of trials you know. No one goes to prison here. (a pause) Here, something is to be named. The presence of the defendants is not a necessary condition.

(The defense counsel falls silent. But he places his hand on the table, as if holding on to this point.)

The case does not wait. Prosecutor.

PROSECUTOR (rises. Fires a single word like an arrow): Responsibility.

(A pause. He wants the word to stand alone.)

Not what was done with the hands. What was accepted with silence. What was justified with "it was complicated." What remained as "they knew but did not say."

Three charges:

One: silence before an injustice they witnessed. Two: distortion of the truth to keep the peace of the home. Three: (he looks at "Me") the transmission of this silence and distortion to the next generation. To one who had the right to know.

(a pause)

But before the charges, the principal witness must say what he saw.

(He sits.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (rises. His jacket a little creased): Before the testimony, one point.

Your Honor. "The transmission of silence" is not a crime. Nowhere in any law is it written that a father and mother must be historians. (a pause) If everyone who stayed silent is guilty, the room empties out.

PROSECUTOR (without rising): Silence before the truth is the transmission of a lie. And a lie to a child is a betrayal of the future.

DEFENSE COUNSEL: "Betrayal." (loud, but restrained) Prosecutor. You are speaking of a father and a mother. Not of a regime. Not of a machine. Two human beings who sat at the spread every night and broke bread.

PROSECUTOR: There. At that same spread. That same bread. And beside it, the things that gathered. Tapes that disappeared. Words left half-said. Voices that should not have been.

(Silence.)

JUDGE: "Me." Tell us what you saw.

("Me" rises. For the first time he looks directly at the two empty chairs. He takes a breath.)

ME: I wanted to be proud of them.

(A pause. This sentence is simple. But weigh it.)

Every time the word "revolution" came up, I did the accounting in my mind. I would say, well, what did my father do? Where was my mother? (a pause) I asked. The answer was always one: "It was complicated."

PROSECUTOR: How many times did you ask?

ME: I don't know. Ten times. Twenty.

PROSECUTOR: And the answer?

ME: The answer did not change. Sometimes "It's not the time." Sometimes "You'll understand later." Sometimes only silence. (a pause) And then another sentence that was repeated more than all the rest: "Mistakes were made."

PROSECUTOR: Only that?

ME: They said mistakes were made. (shorter) Mistakes "were made." The passive voice. No one made them. They were made. Like rain that came. Like wind. Like fate.

(A pause. "Me" sits. A heavy silence in the room.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (quietly, without rising): The passive voice is not the tool of liars. (a pause) The passive voice is the tool of those who do not know who the agent is. Or who know, and are afraid.

(The Judge looks at the counsel. Not approving, not rejecting. Only a look.)

PROSECUTOR (does not rise, only): Fear does not erase the agent.

NARRATOR 1 (to Narrator 2, quietly): "Mistakes were made."

(He laughs, not from joy.)

Do you know since when I have been hearing this sentence?

NARRATOR 2: I know.

NARRATOR 1: Since when?

NARRATOR 2: Since always.

NARRATOR 1: Well, a man wants to put a precise date on it. Say, the Constitutional Revolution. Say, the coup. Say...

NARRATOR 2: It makes no difference.

NARRATOR 1 (as if he did not hear, continues): Every time there was a revolution, "mistakes were made." Every time a war ended, "mistakes were made." Every time blood was spilled and the blood dried and again blood was spilled...

NARRATOR 2: Were made.

(a pause)

NARRATOR 1 (to the audience): Do you know the problem with this verb? It asks no one. (a pause) "Were made" means it is finished. Go home.

JUDGE (without turning his head): Narrators.

NARRATOR 1 (at once): We know, we know.

(Both fall silent. But Narrator 2 has a faint smile that does not fade.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (rises. He does not put his hands in his pockets, not this time):

Your Honor.

Let me say something the prosecutor may also accept:

These two people... (he does not gesture to the empty chairs. He knows everyone knows where they are) ...were probably not wicked.

PROSECUTOR (quickly, from his place): No one said they were wicked.

DEFENSE COUNSEL: Then what exactly is the charge? That they were weak? (a pause) Prosecutor, you expect heroism from human beings.

PROSECUTOR: I expect honesty from human beings. With their child. That is all.

DEFENSE COUNSEL: Honesty. (a pause) Have you ever sat at night, at the spread, facing your ten-year-old child, and tried to explain how the world broke? (a pause) What do you say? Where do you begin?

(Silence)

PROSECUTOR: With the truth.

DEFENSE COUNSEL (more quietly): The truth of which night? (a pause) The truth is sometimes so large it does not fit in one sentence. And they... (a vague gesture toward the chairs) ...chose the shorter sentence. "It was complicated." (a pause) Maybe it was a lie. Maybe it was the only sentence they knew.

PROSECUTOR: They knew other sentences too. We will get to them.

(Silence. The defense counsel stands a moment. Sits.)

Act Two: The Testimony

(The light changes a little, not darker, but heavier. "Me" sits where he was. The prosecutor does not rise. The distance between them is the same. But the air has changed, as if the room has grown smaller.)

JUDGE: "Me." Continue.

("Me" raises his head. But he does not look at the Judge. He looks at a point between the two empty chairs, at a space that is and is not.)

ME (quietly, as if talking to himself): Where do I begin? (a pause) From which night? (a pause) It was not one night. It was... (a pause) it was the air of the house.

(Silence. No one asks a question. "Me" continues on his own, like a man talking out of a dream.)

When my aunt came, Aunt Maryam, the house changed. As if a window opened. There was the sound of laughter. She used to record tapes for us. Banan. Marzieh. The voices from before us.

(a pause)

After my aunt left, my mother would gather up the tapes.

(A pause. The room is silent.)

Quietly. Like someone clearing the dishes. Like someone clearing the trash... (a pause. He does not finish.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (quietly): Maybe she was afraid.

ME: Maybe. (a pause) But it was not only the tapes. Once, there was a guest, and Marzieh's voice played from the tape recorder. My father turned the recorder off. Then quietly, as if nothing had happened, he started talking about football. (a pause) No one said anything. The guest neither.

(Silence.)

PROSECUTOR (without rising, quietly): The sound was turned off.

ME: The sound was turned off. Yes. (a pause) It was always this way. Something was loud, they turned it off. Someone said something, they changed it. (a pause) My mother, my mother was a master. In the middle of any argument, she would rise, bring tea. Bring fruit. "Eat, it's getting cold." (a pause) Her voice was kind. It was always kind. (a pause) It was that very kindness that sat in the place of an answer.

(Narrator 2 nods slowly. Narrator 1 looks at him but says nothing.)

ME: My aunt came once. She said things, about what had happened to her friend's husband. (a pause) In the middle of her words my mother rose and went to the kitchen. My father sat in silence. Then my aunt too fell silent.

(a pause)

After my aunt left, I heard. From the next room. (a pause) My mother said: "She shouldn't say these things in our house anymore." (a pause) My father said: "We shouldn't let them do that to him. (a pause) But..." (a pause) The sentence did not finish.

(A long silence.)

PROSECUTOR (quietly, not harsh, only precise): "We shouldn't let them." (a pause) So they knew.

ME: They knew. (a pause) But that "but," that "but" never finished.

DEFENSE COUNSEL (from his place): They were afraid.

ME (does not look at him): Maybe. (a pause) But fear was only part of it. The other part... (a pause) ...was belief. The belief that if you stay silent, everything works out. That if you bring tea and smile and change the subject, the wound heals on its own.

(a pause)

My mother was like that. She dressed every pain with affection. (a pause) No pain ever healed.

(Juror 4, who has been silent until now, slowly leans forward.)

JUROR 4: A dressing.

(Everyone looks at him.)

Not courage. Not a lie. (a pause) A dressing. (a pause) Every night. On every wound. Without ever looking at the wound.

(He sits.)

JUDGE: Continue.

("Me" does not rise. He speaks from where he is, more quietly. As if each sentence comes from somewhere deeper.)

ME: Later, when I grew up, I realized the pattern was everywhere. (a pause) It was not only politics.

(A pause. Long. The prosecutor waits.)

My first marriage. They knew it was a mistake. My mother had said so herself, not to me, to my aunt. I heard it. (a pause) But at the engagement spread, smiles. Congratulations. "May you be happy." (a pause) No one said no.

(a pause)

Then the divorce came. They knew it had to happen. (a pause) They fought it. "It's shameful." "What will people say." "Be patient." (a pause) I was alone. Utterly alone.

(a pause)

The second marriage. This time it was right, and they themselves knew it was right. (a pause) They opposed it. (a pause) Not loudly. Not with a fight. Quietly. Like those same tapes, they gathered it up. "Now is not the time." "Are you sure?" "Think again." (a pause) On the surface, support. Underneath... (a pause) underneath, no.

(Silence. "Me" has his head down.)

PROSECUTOR (quietly): In all three...

ME (raises his head): In all three I was alone. Yes. (a pause) Like those nights at the spread. When I asked a question and my mother brought tea.

NARRATOR 1 (quietly, to Narrator 2): Tea.

NARRATOR 2 (motionless): Tea was always at the ready.

NARRATOR 1: At every spread where a word must not be spoken, there was tea. (a pause) A whole country that drank tea in place of speaking.

(Narrator 2 says nothing. His faint smile, that same smile, does not fade.)

("Me" has fallen silent. The Judge waits. Then:)

JUDGE: Witness. Was there a moment that was... (a pause) ...more direct than these?

("Me" raises his head. He takes a breath. This moment must land right, not dramatic, not plain. Like a piece whose place has been empty for years and now falls into place on its own.)

ME: There were people... (a pause) ...who disappeared. Around us. Not close relatives. People from the neighborhood. People from the distant family. (a pause) The names would come, and then not come.

(a pause)

My father and mother, never... (a pause) never showed hatred. For anyone. For anything. (a pause) Not for those who took. Not for those who were taken. (a pause) Their silence was distributed fairly.

(Narrator 2 nods. Slowly. Like someone who does not approve, only recognizes.)

ME: One night at the dinner table, a name came up. (a pause) A distant relative. They said he had been taken away. (a pause) My mother rose from the spread. Went to the kitchen. There was the sound of water. Only the sound of water.

(a pause)

My father sat in silence. Then, quietly, like someone reporting the weather, he said: "Well. It was complicated. (a pause) His family too, you know already."

(a pause)

He did not say "he was a bad person." (a pause) He did not. But that was it. (a pause) "You know already." That was it.

(Silence. A silence in which history is seated.)

Later I found out that man had done nothing.

PROSECUTOR (does not sit. From his place): Later. (a pause) How many years later?

ME: Ten years. Maybe more.

PROSECUTOR: And in those ten years, did you ask?

ME: Yes.

PROSECUTOR: What did they say?

ME (more quietly): He said "It was a long time ago." He said "It's no use now." (a pause) Once, only once, he said "We didn't know either."

PROSECUTOR: Did they know?

(Silence.)

ME: Yes. (a pause) Like Aunt Maryam. Like the marriage. Like everything. (a pause) They knew. And my mother brought tea.

("Me" sits.)

(Silence in the room. No one speaks.)

JUROR 4 (slowly leans forward): "You know already."

(Everyone looks at him.)

Three words. Behind a table. (a pause) Not ideology. Not a generation. Not the times. (a pause) Three words that do the work of "he was a bad person," without dirtying the hands.

(He sits. As if his work is done.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (quietly): Maybe. But behind those three words was a life. A home. A spread laid out every night.

JUROR 4: (Looks at him a moment. Then:) There was a life. Three words remained.

(Silence.)

NARRATOR 1 (cannot stay silent): "You know already." (a pause) "He was a bad person." (a pause) "It was complicated." (a pause) Do you know the difference between them?

NARRATOR 2 (motionless): There is no difference.

NARRATOR 1: True. (a pause) They all do the same thing: they close the door.

(a pause)

Whoever was killed, "you know already." Whoever went to prison, "it was complicated." Whoever fled...

NARRATOR 2 (quietly): "He was a weak person."

NARRATOR 1 (looks at him): Exactly. (a pause) Whoever left, he was weak. He was bad. He couldn't take it. (a pause. to the audience) Whoever stayed, a hero. Whoever left, a runaway. (he raises his finger) And do you know what happens then? The child of the one who left comes back, after years, not himself, his mind... (gesturing to the chair of "Me") ...and convenes a court.

NARRATOR 2: The bad people come to an end.

(a pause)

The ones who hand out the label do not come to an end.

NARRATOR 1 (looks at Narrator 2): Are you speaking of yourself or of them?

(Narrator 2 does not answer. His faint smile does not fade.)

(A voice from the back row. Everyone turns. Spectator 1 has risen. No one knew he was here.)

SPECTATOR 1: Excuse me, now.

(A pause. The room does not know what to do with this voice.)

I'm not part of all this. But, can I say something?

In our neighborhood it was the same. Forget tapes, sir, photographs! Photos from before the revolution. My mother had hidden the old photos. (a pause) Not thrown them away, mind you, hidden them. Under the mattress. (a pause) She was afraid to keep them. Afraid to throw them out. (a pause) They're still under the mattress.

(a pause)

And the teacher, the one I mentioned. He was in our neighborhood. The kids loved him. One day he wasn't there. That was it. (a pause) His mother came to our door. She kept saying, tell me where he is. What were we to say? We said nothing. (a pause) Then his mother stopped coming.

(a pause)

Here you're talking about tapes and marriages and "you know already." (a little louder) Sir, in our neighborhood we had ten of these. Ten. Shall I say their names?

(The Judge looks at him. He wants to say you are not permitted, but something holds him back.)

JUDGE: Sit down.

SPECTATOR 1 (does not sit): One moment. One moment.

(He looks at "Me")

Sir. "You know already." (a pause) My father used to say the same thing. (a pause) What's the difference between us? The difference is, you left. (a pause) We stayed.

(He sits. Bitter. Without sarcasm.)

(Spectator 2 stirs. Quietly, but it is clear he wants to speak. Finally:)

SPECTATOR 2: It was different back then.

(Spectator 1 turns and looks at him. This look is an old one. They have been having this argument for years.)

SPECTATOR 1: What was different? They took a man. He didn't come back. What was different?

SPECTATOR 2: Well... (a pause) well, there was a war. There was an enemy. We were defending ourselves. I mean, people really believed. God rest him, Haj-Reza's son was seventeen when he went to the front. Seventeen. (a pause) Now whatever happened, that belief wasn't a lie.

SPECTATOR 1: The belief wasn't a lie. (a pause) But the blood that was spilled was real. The teacher who was taken was real. His mother who stopped coming, real. (a pause) And you said nothing either.

SPECTATOR 2: Wasn't America bad? Wasn't there an enemy?

SPECTATOR 1: Man... (patiently, but tired, he has said this a hundred times) there was an enemy. Fine. Was the teacher an enemy? Was the neighbor's child an enemy? (a pause) Was the one who left an enemy?

(A vague gesture toward the chair of "Me")

SPECTATOR 2 (more quietly, defensive): No one said he was an enemy.

SPECTATOR 1: No. (a pause) You said "he was a weak person." (a pause) It's the same thing. Only the clothes have changed.

(Silence. Spectator 2 does not answer. Not from having nothing to say. From the weight of what the next sentence demands.)

SPECTATOR 1 (more quietly): You said it too.

(Silence.)

SPECTATOR 2 (quietly): I don't know. (a pause) Maybe I said it. (a pause) A man, in that atmosphere... (he shakes his head, does not finish)

(The Judge looks. He does not throw them out.)

JUROR 3 (rises. When this one rises, the air changes):

One moment.

(Everyone waits.)

This court speaks of two people. But behind these two people, a whole generation stands.

A generation whose love was real, even if it was blind. We wrote books. We gave speeches. (a pause. this "we" is personal) We gave our lives. We took lives. (a pause) We gathered up tapes, yes. We cut off words, yes. (a pause) The father and mother of this man were not alone. We were all of us there.

(He casts a glance at Spectator 2, as if this unknown man has just confirmed his words.)

And if today we say "it was a mistake"... (a pause) we must know that "we" made the mistake. Not "they." (a pause) We. This very "we" sitting here and passing judgment.

JUROR 5 (cold, without pause): Not being alone in the mistake does not change the mistake.

JUROR 3 (turns toward him): I did not say it changes it. I said see its dimensions.

JUROR 5: Dimensions. (a pause) The tapes were gathered up. The words were cut off. The marriage was ruined. The divorce became loneliness. (a pause) I have seen the dimensions. The mistake is the same mistake.

JUROR 3 (his voice does not rise, but its weight rises): You are right. But a judgment that is only accounting, without understanding... (a pause) is itself a kind of violence.

JUROR 5: Understanding without accounting is itself a kind of escape.

(The tension between them hangs in the air.)

JUROR 6 (without rising, quietly, like someone drawing a boundary line): Let us not mix two things. (a pause) One is "why this happened," that is history. The other is "are these two responsible," that is morality. (a pause) History does not answer to morality. Morality does not escape from history. But the two are not the same thing.

(Jurors 3 and 5 both look at him. He does not continue. His point is made.)

(Juror 1, who has been silent until now, quietly, like someone speaking from another depth:)

JUROR 1: A root that rots under the soil, the branches fall later. (a pause) First the root. Then the branch. Then a fruit no one eats.

(A pause. Everyone looks at him. He does not continue.)

DEFENSE COUNSEL (rises. This time he works with his hands, as if he wants to shape something in the air):

Your Honor.

I do not say they are innocent. (a pause) I said this from the start and I say it again.

But let me paint a scene.

It is night. The spread is laid. The child is seated. (a pause) A name has come from the radio, the name of someone who is no longer. Or a cassette has been turned on with a voice that should not be. Or the aunt has said something. (a pause) The mother must answer. The father must answer. (a pause) What are they to say?

That the world is unjust? To a ten-year-old child? (a pause) That they themselves knew and did nothing? (a pause) That that man had done nothing and only had bad luck?

(Silence)

I say people sometimes go blind out of love. Not out of wickedness. And one who has gone blind out of love... (a pause) when he sees, the seeing is heavier for him than for anyone else.

Because he must accept that what he lived for came to this.

PROSECUTOR (without rising): Came to what?

DEFENSE COUNSEL (a pause. This question struck exactly where it should not have): To... (a pause) to a child who has left and convened a court from the other side of the world.

(Silence)

Some people bear this weight. Some do not.

This is weakness. But weakness is not a crime.

(He sits.)

PROSECUTOR (rises. Short, without ceremony):

"Love is blinder than fear." (a pause) I accept that.

But the question is not why they went blind. The question is, when the eyes opened... (he looks at "Me") ...what did they do?

The child asked. Ten times. Twenty times. (a pause) The answer did not change.

The tapes were gathered up. The words were cut off. The marriage was ruined and the mother brought tea. (a pause) Every time, the same dressing.

(a pause)

Any other sentence, "I don't know," "I was wrong," "I was afraid," each one required some courage. (a pause) "You know already" requires no courage. It requires only the sacrifice of one other person.

(He sits.)

(A long silence.)

ME (without rising, quietly, as if talking to himself): "I don't know." (a pause) If only once they had said it. (a pause) If only once my mother, instead of tea... (a pause) had just sat down and said: I don't know.

(The Judge looks at him.)

JUDGE: Continue.

ME: No. (a pause) That was all.

JUDGE (this time he turns his head): Narrators. For the last time.

NARRATOR 1 (raises his hands): We surrender.

(Narrator 2 says nothing. His silence is a kind of protest.)

NARRATOR 1 (quietly, to Narrator 2): Don't you want to say something?

NARRATOR 2 (a moment's pause. Then, to the stage, not to the audience): "I don't know" was a lie too.

(a pause)

They knew.

(a pause)

They knew from the aunt. They knew from the tape. They knew from the marriage. (a pause) Everywhere they knew, and everywhere they brought tea.

(Narrator 1 looks at him. This time he does not laugh.)

Act Three: The Verdict

(The light changes. Not darker, brighter. But a cold brightness. "Me" rises from his chair. He goes to the two empty chairs. He stands before them. Facing them.)

ME: I wanted to be proud of you.

(A pause. This is the sentence that was spoken in Act One too. But this time it is different. The first time, it was a wish. Now, an accounting.)

I wanted to say that my father... (a pause) my father was a man who stood in the hardest years. My mother was a woman who saw the truth and did not stay silent. (a pause) These are the things I wanted to say.

(a pause)

I cannot.

(a pause)

You gathered up the tapes. You cut off the words. You silenced the aunt. You knew, and you said "you know already." (a pause) When the marriage was a mistake, silence. When the divorce was right, opposition. When the marriage was right, again opposition. (a pause) And every time I asked, my mother brought tea.

(a pause)

I do not accept your choices. I do not accept your account. I will not deny my own pain so that your peace may be kept.

(A pause. He takes a breath.)

You had the right to be afraid. (a pause) You did not have the right to put affection in the place of an answer. (a pause) Affection without truth... (a pause) is that same dressing on a wound that opens again every night.

(a pause)

But I will no longer let my whole life be spent on your endless trial.

(He turns toward his chair. He does not sit. He stands.)

JUDGE (rises. From this moment the tone changes, not louder, deeper):

Jury.

(The six rise.)

Do the defendants bear moral responsibility?

JUROR 5: They are responsible.

(Silence.)

JUROR 4: They are responsible.

(a pause)

Excuse and responsibility live together. They do not cancel each other.

JUROR 6: They are responsible.

(a pause)

But responsibility has different forms. (a pause) If we equate "responsibility" with "wickedness," we have neither done justice nor understood. (a pause) These people were afraid. Out of fear they were silent. They covered their silence with affection. From their covering, someone was harmed. (a pause) This is responsibility. It is not wickedness.

JUROR 3: They are responsible, alongside a generation that must all answer. (a pause) And I... (he gestures to himself) I too.

JUROR 1 (quietly, like someone pulling something out of the soil): They are responsible. (a pause) But the verdict is not for them. (a pause) It needs to be seen, for "Me" himself. So that he knows where the root rotted. And knows that his own branch is still alive.

JUROR 2 (the last one. He casts a glance at the two empty chairs): They are responsible. And what was taken from "Me" does not return. The verdict is not restitution. It is only naming.

(The six sit.)

(From the back row, Spectator 2, quietly, more tired than before:)

SPECTATOR 2: We thought...

(He cannot finish.)

We thought we were... (a pause) we were doing right... (a pause)

(He puts his hand on his knees. His head is down.)

SPECTATOR 1 (quietly, without sarcasm): I know.

(Spectator 2 raises his head. Looks at him.)

SPECTATOR 1: I know what you want to say. (a pause) I was there too.

(He puts his hand on the other's shoulder. A moment. Then takes it away.)

(Silence. The Judge picks up a cup of tea, it is cold, that is clear, looks at it a moment. Then quietly sets it down. He looks at a place the stage does not have. Then he speaks. Quietly. Like someone who has said these sentences many times, but they are still heavy for him.)

JUDGE: The verdict of the court:

The defendants are not absolved of moral responsibility. The suffering that reached "Me" from their choices is real. The anger of "Me" is legitimate.

(His gaze falls on "Me.")

But "Me" is not condemned to remain their jailer in his own mind for a lifetime. "Me" can see the truth, condemn it, set a distance...

(a pause, hold this pause)

...and go.

(He stands a moment. Then:)

The case leaves the hands of blind anger and is given to the hands of awareness. Not forgetting. Not acquittal. A drawing of boundaries.

(He looks at the cup, that cold cup. He does not pick it up. He sits.)

(Everyone rises. "Me" is the last. He goes toward the door. A moment, only a moment, he looks at the two empty chairs. Then he goes.)

(The door of the room closes.)

(The prosecutor and defense counsel and jury and judge leave. One by one. Without a sound. The spectators rise too, quietly, without a word. Spectator 1 goes ahead. Spectator 2 lingers a moment, looks at the two empty chairs, then lowers his head and goes. The narrators remain.)

NARRATOR 1 (looks at the judge's empty cup): He left.

NARRATOR 2: He left.

NARRATOR 1: Is that good?

(Narrator 2 says nothing. He looks at the two empty chairs.)

NARRATOR 1: You mean he left and that's it? (a pause) He drew a boundary and left?

NARRATOR 2: He left. (a pause) He left again.

NARRATOR 1: And tomorrow night?

(Narrator 2 looks at him. A faint smile.)

NARRATOR 1 (continues, a little more tired): Maybe the next generation won't build this room.

NARRATOR 2 (quietly, without emphasis): They will build it.

NARRATOR 1: Why?

NARRATOR 2: Because they still gather up tapes. (a pause) They still say "you know already." (a pause) They still bring tea.

(Narrator 1 stands a moment. Then he puts on his jacket and goes. Without farewell.)

(Narrator 2 remains. The lights go out one by one. The last light falls on the two empty chairs.)

(Narrator 2 goes toward them. He stands before one. He raises his hand, as if to touch something. He does not. He lowers his hand.)

(Darkness.)

(Only a sound, again the same sound as the beginning: a chair dragged across the floor.)

End

"The verdict is not restitution. It is only naming."
