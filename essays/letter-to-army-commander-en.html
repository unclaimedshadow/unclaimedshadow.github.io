An Open Letter to the Commander-in-Chief of Iran's Army

1404/12/10

1 Esfand 1404: the day Iran is under bombs, and its army is silent

Commander,

I will not stand on ceremony. There is no time for ceremony. The country is burning.

What happened

The ninth of Esfand, three in the morning, the sky over Tehran lit up. Not from Nowruz fireworks. From bombs. Israel and America struck together. Israel's codename was "Roaring Lion." America's was "Epic Wrath." They did not even trouble to name it in Persian. They named it in their own tongue and translated it into the language of bombs.

Khamenei was killed. In his office. Forty other commanders were killed with him. The nuclear sites were hit. The missile bases were hit. Twenty-four provinces were drawn in.

Two hundred and one civilians died. Seven hundred and forty-seven were wounded. The very people the system was supposed to protect.

And the Revolutionary Guard, guardian of the revolution, defender of the shrine, conqueror of Karbala-5, what did it do?

What the Guard did

It fired missiles. But not at Israel. Not at the American bases. At Bahrain. At the Emirates. At Qatar. At Kuwait. At Jordan.

Let me say it plainly: the Revolutionary Guard, in answer to the bombing of Iranian soil, bombed the soil of five neighboring countries.

Saudi Arabia issued a statement and said it stands with its neighbors. Dubai's airport closed. Jordan sounded its sirens. Tens of millions of Arabs who had no quarrel with Iran yesterday became our enemies today. Not because of Israel. Because of the Guard.

Then they came and said: we struck twenty-seven American bases. America did not confirm it. They showed no footage. They brought no proof. They just said it. As always. As for forty years.

They closed the Strait of Hormuz. They took the world's oil hostage. Now every ship that cannot pass the strait makes Iran one more enemy.

Commander, you know war. You understand tactics. Tell me: is this fighting, or national suicide?

What the army knows, and stays silent about

You know, better than any politician, better than any cleric, better than any Guard general, that:

One: Iran has no air defense. The Russian S-300, which we begged for over ten years, could not stop a single one of their cursed F's and B's and C's, whatever you want to call them. You know this. Your pilots know this. Every senior officer on the joint staff knows this.

Two: Iran's air force flies F-14s and Mirages and Phantoms that belong to decades ago. Your pilots are the bravest men in this country. But you cannot fight with museum aircraft. You know this better than I do.

Three: The Revolutionary Guard has swallowed this country's military budget. Billions of dollars went to Hezbollah, to the Houthis, to Hamas, to the Hashd. What was left for Iran's army, the real army, the national army? A pilot with no plane. A gunner with no shells. A captain with no destroyer.

Four: For forty years the Guard has kept the army deliberately weak. Why? Because a strong army is dangerous, not to the enemy, but to the regime. The Guard fears the army. It fears the Babak, the Abbas, the colonel and brigadier and general who thinks. They kept the army weak so the Guard would stay strong. And now look at the result: the Guard could not defend the country either. It could not, and it would not let the army do it.

You know all this. So why are you silent?

The oath you have forgotten

Every officer of Iran's army, when he takes his rank, swears an oath. An oath to the soil of Iran. An oath to the people of Iran.

My question is this: was that oath to the people, or to the regime?

To the woman and child sitting under the bombs, or to the cleric on the pulpit? To the schoolchild crushed in a classroom, or to the general boasting on television?

This is not an abstract question. Today its answer is a matter of action.

If the oath was to the people, the people are dying.

What must be done

I am not telling you to stage a coup. A coup is a soldier's disgrace, and you know this better than I do.

I am telling you to stand.

What does it mean to stand?

It means the army issues a statement and says: we protect the people of Iran. Not the system. Not the ideology. Not the regional adventures. The people.

It means the army stands in the way of the war the Guard is starting with the neighbors. A war for which Iran has neither the budget, nor the weapons, nor the allies. A war that only kills the people's children and accomplishes nothing.

It means the army opens the Strait of Hormuz. Not for America's sake. For Iran's sake. Because every day the strait is closed, Iran's economy steps closer to death and we find one more enemy.

It means the army protects the people, in the streets, in the cities, in the hard days ahead. Not with repression. With protection.

History has its eyes on you

Commander, Iran's army is heir to the men who took back Khorramshahr. With their nails. With their blood. With nothing. The world was finished with you, and you stood.

That same army, that same blood, that same honor, where is it now? Sitting and watching? Watching what? Watching a handful of madmen drag the country into the pit of hell?

Look. Look around you:

The people have been in the streets since Azar. The largest protests since the revolution. The rial is no longer money. It is paper. The young are fleeing. The minds have gone. Industry is dead. Agriculture is dead. There is no water. There is no power. And now, the bombing.

And what is the regime's answer? More missiles. More slogans. More manufactured enemies.

This is no longer government. This is collective suicide. And the army is watching.

A last word

Commander, I am not asking you to be a hero. Heroics belong to the Guard.

I am asking you to be a soldier.

A soldier of Iran. Not a soldier of the regime.

History asks one thing of you. One sentence:

"The army of Iran stands with the people of Iran."

That is all.

That one sentence changes Iran. Not with a bullet, with a word. Not with war, with standing firm.

Eighty million people are waiting. Their children are buried under rubble. Their homes are burned. And they are looking at you.

If you do not stand now, when will you stand?

If you do not stand, who will?

Zahhak died. But his serpents are alive, and they still feed on the brains of this land's young.

Where is that Kaveh? When will that lion roar again, and the sun shine on the Ahuran soil of Iran?

An Iranian, from within the fire
