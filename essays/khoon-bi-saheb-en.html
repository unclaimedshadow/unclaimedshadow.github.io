Ownerless Blood

Patriotism in a land that buries its dead by night

1404/12/12

We are a strange nation. We have memory, but our memory has a switch. And the switch is not in our own hands. Someone drops a bomb from outside, the memory turns on. Someone fires a bullet from inside, the memory turns off. It is as if our brains have a censor: an internal state broadcaster.

Do you remember Dey 1404? Just two months ago?

The people poured into the streets. The regime poured onto the people. Not with batons. With bullets. Direct fire. Thirty thousand in forty-eight hours. Thirty thousand. Write the number on paper and look at it. Then remember Babi Yar, that catastrophe at which, eighty years later, the world still lowers its head in shame. Dey 1404 went past even that.

They took the bodies away by night. Fathers are still calling out: Sepehr, my boy, where are you? Mothers are still searching, from one hospital to another, from one cemetery to another. The smell of blood is still under the asphalt, but no one holds their nose. They cut the internet so the world would not see, and so we would not see ourselves. And we, too, did not want to see.

Fine. Now the question:

Where were you?

You, the very ones now anxious about the American-Israeli birds of prey. You, the very ones posting hashtags and flags and shedding tears. When they killed thirty thousand people, not foreigners, our own, where were you? What did you write? What did you say?

Or, and this is the harder question: did you care?

The name of this disease

Every pain needs a name. I will call this one conditional patriotism.

What does that mean? It means a patriotism that only works when the enemy is foreign. A bomb falls from the sky, and the blood boils. An American missile lands, and it is "Oh, Iran! Oh, soil of the homeland!" But when that same soil is crushed under a Guardsman's boot? Nothing. Silence. Absolute silence. National silence.

Let us review the history of this silence:

The year '67: thousands of political prisoners executed. Silence. Aban '98: protesters machine-gunned in the streets. Silence. They killed Mahsa Amini, then hundreds of other young people. Silence. Dey 1404: a catastrophe whose number passed Babi Yar. Silence.

But the moment the first American missile hit Tehran, everyone suddenly woke up. Suddenly Iran became sacred. Suddenly the soil became dear. Suddenly the blood boiled.

Sir, this soil was the same soil two months ago. The blood of thirty thousand was on it. Was it not dear then? Was that blood not Iranian blood? Or does Iranian blood only have color when the bullet that spilled it is American?

Fifty years of blood: the accounting

Let us count without ceremony. Numbers and figures. Without emotion:

From '57 to today, the Islamic Republic has struck Iran harder than any foreign enemy. This is not a slogan. Do the math:

The executions of '67. The massacre of the Kurds. The persecution of the Baha'is. The chain murders. Kahrizak. Aban '98. Dey 1404. The prison and torture of writers and journalists and lawyers and students. An eight-year war in which, after two years, a ceasefire resolution came, and Khomeini said no, and sent the young to die for six more years. The brain drain: millions of educated Iranians who fled and are now building other countries. The destruction of the economy. The destruction of the environment. Lake Urmia dried up. The Zayandeh-rud died. Hamoun was gone. The wetlands ran out.

Now add it up. The human, cultural, economic, environmental toll.

Then compare it with what America and Israel did in these few days.

Then ask yourself: which enemy has been the greater one? The one that came from outside and dropped bombs for a few days, or the one that for fifty years ate and ate and ate from within?

Conditional patriots: you know these people

The first kind: they did not see. These are easier to forgive. For forty years their news was the state broadcaster. They honestly believe the regime has been the guardian of Iran. They are victims of propaganda, not accomplices in the crime. But my friend, it is 1404. There is satellite. There is the internet, even filtered. Ignorance in the information age is no longer an excuse. It is the duty of every Iranian to know what has befallen their compatriot.

The second kind: they saw and said nothing. These are a separate account. For years they saw. They heard. They understood. But each time they had a fresh excuse: "It is not the time." "The situation is complicated." "The opposition has problems too." "Let it not get worse." But the moment a foreign bomb fell, they suddenly became brave. Suddenly their blood boiled. Why? Because anti-American patriotism is cheap. It is free. It costs nothing. No one goes to prison for it, no one loses their job. But anti-Islamic-Republic patriotism? That has a price. And these people are not the kind to pay a price.

The third kind: the fishermen. Professional opportunists. Whichever way the wind blows, their sail is set that way. Yesterday they were "reformists." Today they have become "resistance." Tomorrow they will be whatever else carries the least risk and the most profit. These are not patriots. They are merchants. Patriotism, too, is a business for them.

What patriotism means: a simple definition

Patriotism means you feel the pain of your compatriot. It does not matter who caused that pain. It does not matter whether you are sitting by the Pacific shore in Los Angeles or at the end of the line in Naziabad in a forty-square-meter apartment.

If you only weep when a foreign bomb falls, but stay silent when your own government fires bullets at the people, you are not a patriot. You are an anti-foreigner. And these two things differ as the earth from the sky.

A patriot is someone who sees Iranian blood as Iranian. Whether the bullet came from a Guardsman's rifle or from an F-35.

So what is to be done?

First, reclaim the memory. No one has the right to forget Dey 1404. No foreign crisis is an excuse to wipe that blood from memory. Though, and let me be blunt, the writer of these lines does not regard the American-Israeli attack as a catastrophe. The catastrophe was the one that fell two months ago. By our own hands. On ourselves.

Second, reject the false binary. Anyone who says "either you are with the Islamic Republic or with America" is either a fool or a deceiver. One can be against both. Not only can one be, this is the only honorable position.

Third, separate patriotism from regime-worship. Iran is not the Islamic Republic. Iran existed before this regime. It will exist after it too. To defend Iran is to defend its people, not to defend a regime that for fifty years has spilled the blood of those very people. Anyone who treats these two as one is either deceiving himself or wants to deceive you.

Fourth, keep the names of the dead alive. Record the names of the victims of Dey 1404. Write their stories. Do not let them fade. Regimes feed on the forgetfulness of the people. The greatest thing you can do for your homeland is this: remember.

Fifth, start with yourself. If you were silent until yesterday, confess it. Set aside your excuses. We have all been caught in this pain for long years. To confess the pain is the first step of the cure.

Last word

I endorse neither the American attack, though in my heart of hearts I am glad of it, nor the Islamic Republic. I have no doubt that the Islamic Republic and the Guard are the enemies of Iran. The internal enemy. And the one who strikes from within is the more dangerous, because it wears the clothes of one of our own, because it kills in the name of God, because it has had fifty years and used that time to destroy every structure of a nation.

But to tell you the truth, do you know why it is hard to arrive at this? Because one realizes that he too, in places, was an accomplice in their crime. In those years, when I heard of the crimes of the sixties, out of ignorance I did not believe it. When I saw the exile of the Los Angeles Iranians, in my heart I laughed at them. When I heard the pain of the Kurdish youth, I just slapped the label of separatist on him and moved on. I know that, with my mind, I was once an accomplice of the Guard and the Islamic Republic. But no longer. With all my being, no. And this is a hard moment, as if you have to jump from a height not knowing what the ground looks like. Fear. Fear.

And to those who have suddenly become patriots, I say: patriotism is not easy. Patriotism is not selective. If you only love your homeland when a foreigner strikes it, you do not love your homeland. You hate the foreigner. And that is not enough.

Patriotism is a heavy burden. The late Beyzai always reminded us: when you speak of homeland and history, remember that you place a heavy weight of responsibility on your own shoulders. Otherwise, talk is cheap.

Iran needs full-time patriots. Not seasonal ones.

But until that day, if it comes, the mothers of Dey are still going from one cemetery to another, looking for the graves of their children. Alone. Like the mothers of Khavaran, if any breath is left in them. In silence. As always.
