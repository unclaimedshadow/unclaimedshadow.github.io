Iran Science, Technology and Innovation Strategy 2026-2040

Why Grand Plans Fail and What to Do Instead

1404/12/04

Grand strategies for national development share a family resemblance with horoscopes: broad enough to sound right, vague enough to never be proven wrong, specific enough to give the reader the illusion of a plan. Iran has produced at least ten science and technology strategy documents in two decades. Each named the correct goals: knowledge-based economy, technological self-sufficiency, regional leadership. None of them worked. The question is not whether the goals were right. The question is whether the approach was designed to survive contact with reality.

This document takes a different approach. It does not begin with what Iran should become by 2040. It begins with what Iran actually is in early 2026: what breaks it, what has survived, and what kind of system might gain strength from the very shocks that have weakened the country so far. It operates under two scenarios that most Iranian policy documents have been too timid to address openly: one in which the Islamic Republic continues to govern, and one in which it does not.

Small interventions outperform grand designs. The countries that thrive after crises are almost never the ones that followed the most detailed plan. Large centralized programs fail not because they lack ambition, but because they lack humility. Humility before uncertainty is the single quality most absent from Iran's previous strategies.

I. What Iran Actually Is: An Honest Inventory

Every useful strategy begins with an inventory honest enough to be uncomfortable. Iran's inventory reveals not a paradox but a diagnosis: impressive inputs, mediocre outputs.

Start with what works. Iran ranks 64th on the 2024 Global Innovation Index, up from the lower ranks it occupied a decade ago (WIPO, 2024). Over fifty science and technology parks. More than ten thousand registered knowledge-based companies with combined sales reported above twenty-five billion dollars. Roughly seventy-five thousand indexed scientific publications per year, placing Iran around fifteenth globally in citation output (SCImago, 2024). Over a thousand researchers among the world's top one percent most-cited scholars (Clarivate, 2024). A nanotechnology program once ranked fourth worldwide, now slipping as India and South Korea invest harder.

These numbers are real. They measure activity, not impact. A country can publish seventy-five thousand papers a year and still have farmers in South Khorasan trucking water because their wells ran dry. The distance between a published paper and a working solution is not a gap that more papers will close. It is a structural disconnection between the people who produce knowledge and the people who need it applied.

The military sector proves Iran can close this gap when it chooses to. The Shahed drone program produced UAVs with estimated ranges of one thousand to twenty-five hundred kilometers at widely cited unit costs between twenty and fifty thousand dollars, though actual transaction prices vary by buyer. These were exported to Russia, Ethiopia, Sudan, Bolivia, Venezuela, and Tajikistan, and supplied to allied groups across the region (Atlantic Council, 2024; Army Technology, 2025). In January 2025, the Iranian Army received over a thousand new strategic UAVs with AI-driven target identification (Jerusalem Post, January 2025). The IRGC Navy operates over a thousand fast attack craft in the Persian Gulf, trained for coordinated swarm tactics that have challenged far more expensive naval platforms (Naval News, 2024; CNAS, 2024). Iran has also built a domestic surveillance infrastructure: facial recognition systems, the "Nazer" citizen-reporting application, an estimated fifty thousand police body cameras with live-streaming, AI-powered monitoring platforms (Iran Wire, 2025; Forbes, 2024; Iran International, 2025).

The moral judgment on these capabilities is not complicated. A country that deploys artificial intelligence to identify women who remove their headscarves instead of to predict earthquakes has made a choice that is both stupid and cruel. But the strategic observation is separate from the moral one: when the Iranian state treats something as a genuine priority, it can build things that work at scale. The state has proven it can execute. It has never proven it can execute for the benefit of ordinary people.

Then came the war. In June 2025, the Twelve-Day War between Iran and Israel, with the United States joining on June 22 under Operation Midnight Hammer, destroyed much of the military-industrial infrastructure described above. Israeli air strikes beginning June 13 targeted Natanz, the Isfahan Nuclear Technology Center, ballistic missile sites, and energy facilities. US B-2 stealth bombers followed, dropping bunker-busting munitions on Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan. The IAEA confirmed that nearly all sensitive equipment at Fordow had been destroyed. A ceasefire was announced June 24. Iran's drone and missile inventory was substantially depleted.

The distinction that matters: the hardware was destroyed; the people who built it were not. The engineers, metallurgists, software developers, and systems integrators are still in Iran, or in the diaspora, or both. Destroyed hardware is a loss. Intact human capital is an option. The question is what that option gets used for.

On the liability side, the picture has worsened sharply. R&D spending hovers around 0.7 to 0.8 percent of GDP, well below the global average of roughly two percent, less than half of what Turkey spends (World Bank data). Most of that comes from the state, meaning little market discipline on what gets funded. The World Bank projects GDP will contract by 2.8 percent in 2026. Inflation: between 42 and 60 percent. The rial: approximately 1.5 million per dollar on the unofficial market. Food prices: seventy percent higher than the previous year. Parliament voted in 2025 to remove four zeros from the national currency, a cosmetic fix for a structural collapse.

The research-to-economy connection remains broken in the same way it was broken a decade ago. The 2016 UNCTAD Science, Technology and Innovation Policy Review identified fragmented governance, weak competition, and a missing intermediation layer between research and enterprise. Ten years later, the same problems persist. Professors get rewarded for publications, not for solving problems someone will pay for. Firms say academic research rarely addresses their actual needs.

The starting position: Iran has scientific talent, industrial experience, geographic centrality, and ninety million people. It also has a shattered military infrastructure, a collapsing economy, a governance system that rewards loyalty over competence, and a generation of its best-trained citizens leaving as fast as they can. The question is not whether Iran has capability. The question is whether that capability can be redirected before the people who carry it are gone.

II. Why Twenty Years of Plans Failed: A Deeper Diagnosis

The standard explanation focuses on execution failures: overlapping agencies, weak coordination, underfunding. These are real problems, but they are symptoms. The underlying disease is more fundamental: Iran's planning system is built on assumptions about how economies develop that are wrong.

Wrong assumption one: you can plan your way to innovation. Every previous Iranian strategy reads like an architect's blueprint: identify the sectors, set the targets, allocate the resources, build the institutions, wait for results. This works for things you already know how to do (building a highway, expanding a port, training nurses). It fails for things nobody knows how to do yet, which is what innovation actually is. You cannot schedule a discovery. You cannot set a five-year target for a breakthrough. You can only create conditions in which discoveries become more likely, then let people who are closer to the problems than any central planner find solutions no central planner would have imagined.

Wrong assumption two: the government should pick winners. Iran's innovation policy has consistently selected sectors, selected companies, and channeled resources to them. This concentrates risk instead of spreading it. When the government picks a winner and the winner fails, the resources are gone. When the winner succeeds, the winner becomes dependent on government favor rather than on delivering value to customers. The countries that built lasting innovation capacity, including the very examples Iran's planners love citing (South Korea, Taiwan, Israel), did not succeed because the government picked the right sectors. They succeeded because the government created conditions under which hundreds of private actors could try hundreds of things. Most failed. A few succeeded beyond anyone's expectations. TSMC did not emerge from a plan to dominate semiconductors. It emerged from a bet on a business model, the pure-play foundry, that most industry analysts considered dubious.

Wrong assumption three: trust can be built from the top down. Iran's quality problems in pharmaceuticals, engineering services, and industrial goods are not primarily technical problems. They are trust problems. When a hospital in Baghdad considers an Iranian biosimilar, the purchasing decision depends on whether the buyer believes the clinical trial data, whether the manufacturing facility meets international standards, and whether after-sales support will actually be there. A government decree cannot create these things. Trust is built transaction by transaction, over years, through repeated demonstrations of reliability. One verified failure, or worse one case of fraud, can destroy a decade of trust-building. Illicit Iranian-made medicines have been found in both Iraq and Afghanistan (Iran International, 2024). This is not a footnote. It is an existential threat to the entire export proposition.

Wrong assumption four, the most destructive: nothing should ever be stopped. In every functioning innovation system, roughly thirty percent of publicly funded projects are terminated, restructured, or redirected within five years. This is not failure. This is learning. Killing what does not work frees resources for what might. Iran's system almost never formally terminates anything. Projects do not end; they fade. They lose momentum and funding but keep their offices, staff, and overhead. One engineer at a major Iranian research university described it to a foreign reviewer as "a museum of good intentions." The inability to stop things is not a bureaucratic quirk. It is a direct consequence of a political system in which accountability flows upward toward loyalty and downward toward blame, and no one benefits from admitting that something they approved did not work.

Wrong assumption five: Iran is one geography. A water-technology firm in Kerman faces fundamentally different conditions than one in East Azerbaijan. A digital startup in Tehran inhabits a different labor market than one in Mashhad. A village near Zahedan where families walk two kilometers for water and a neighborhood in north Tehran where residents complain about low water pressure are not the same problem. National plans that apply identical incentives, identical forms, and identical targets across thirty-one provinces are not plans. They are fictions.

These five failures explain why Iran has generated impressive input statistics (parks, papers, patents, registered companies) without generating corresponding improvements in the daily lives of its people. The visible benefit of each plan was political: the appearance of progress. The hidden cost was the slow strangulation of everything that actually works.

III. Two Scenarios: The Regime Stays, The Regime Goes

This document addresses a reality most Iranian policy documents avoid: the Islamic Republic may not survive to see 2040. It is dishonest to write a fifteen-year strategy while pretending the political structure is permanent. It is equally dishonest to write one that only works after a transition that may never happen. Every proposal here is designed to be useful under both scenarios.

Scenario A: The Islamic Republic persists. The regime survives through repression, economic management, and the absence of a viable alternative. The IRGC's economic empire remains intact. Sanctions continue or tighten. International isolation deepens. The brain drain accelerates. The strategy must work with a shrinking budget, a hostile international environment, and a governance system in which merit-based allocation competes with patronage, corruption, and security-state interference.

Scenario B: A political transition occurs. The Islamic Republic gives way to a successor government through internal evolution, negotiated transition, or popular pressure. The events of 2025 and 2026 have made this more plausible than at any point since 1979. Khamenei is eighty-six and frail. The Twelve-Day War destroyed much of the regime's military deterrent. The economy is in freefall. In January 2026, the state responded to nationwide protests with a crackdown of unprecedented lethality: security forces killed thousands of unarmed civilians in what Amnesty International called the deadliest crackdown in post-revolutionary history. The death toll remains contested: the government reported 3,117; HRANA confirmed at least 6,506, including 216 children; Iran International, citing leaked IRGC intelligence reports, put the figure above 36,500. The wide range reflects the total information blackout imposed during the killings. What is not contested: the response was lethal, systematic, and directed at unarmed people.

A government that shoots its own children in the streets is not going to transform itself into a transparent innovation system. But it is generating the conditions for its own replacement. Analysts across the spectrum now discuss not whether a transition will occur, but when and how (Atlantic Council, 2026; Hudson Institute, 2026; Brookings, 2026).

Under Scenario B, the landscape transforms. Sanctions relief, based on precedents from Iraq (2003), Myanmar (2012), and Sudan (2017), could begin within months. Foreign direct investment into a ninety-million-person market with the world's fourth-largest proven oil reserves would be substantial. World Bank and IMF engagement would bring both capital and reform expertise. Technology licensing from Europe, Japan, and South Korea would open. Three to five million diaspora Iranians, many of them the engineers, physicians, and entrepreneurs the country needs, would face a credible reason to return or invest for the first time.

The design principle is simple: every proposal works under Scenario A and works better, faster, and at larger scale under Scenario B. Nothing here requires waiting for political change. Everything benefits from it.

IV. What Not to Do: The Power of Removal

The most effective way to improve a system is usually not to add something new but to remove something harmful. A doctor who stops prescribing a drug that is making the patient sicker does more good than one who adds a new drug on top. This applies directly to Iran's science and technology system, where the highest-impact reforms are not new programs but the removal of existing obstacles.

Stop treating publication count as the measure of scientific success. Iran's universities incentivize faculty to publish in quantity rather than solve problems. The result: seventy-five thousand papers a year and a documented integrity crisis. Multiple studies have found Iran among the countries with the highest retraction rates relative to publication volume; one analysis put Iran's retraction proportion at 15.52 per ten thousand publications, the highest among countries studied (Hesselmann et al., 2019; Malekpour et al., 2022). Paper mills, citation manipulation, and predatory publishing are not fringe phenomena. They are the predictable consequence of a system that rewards the appearance of knowledge rather than the fact of it. The fix is not another integrity committee. The fix is to change what gets rewarded: tie faculty evaluation, tenure, and salary to measurable deployment outcomes, a water utility that works better, a hospital that uses a tool the professor's lab developed. Publication remains useful as a record. It stops being the measure of worth.

Stop protecting failing projects from termination. Every publicly funded project should have a defined lifespan, specific milestones, and a termination trigger. Miss two consecutive milestones without an approved redesign, and the project ends. Resources go to the next proposal. The current practice, in which projects never formally die but retain staff, office space, and overhead while producing nothing, is a tax on everything that does work.

Stop forcing innovators through bureaucratic obstacle courses. A young engineer in Isfahan who wants to start a technology company faces registration processes, multiple government approvals, tax complexity, and regulations written for large state enterprises. Every hour spent on paperwork is an hour not spent building a product. The fix is not adding a "fast track" alongside the old system. That just creates two systems. Eliminate requirements that serve no demonstrated purpose.

Stop restricting internet access. A country that imposes internet shutdowns, filters major global platforms, and blocks its citizens from standard cloud services, version-control platforms, and AI training frameworks is amputating its own hands and wondering why it cannot build. The same state that demonstrated sophisticated capability in satellite signal jamming and AI surveillance has not channeled equivalent effort into the digital infrastructure its citizens need for productive work. Under Scenario A, full internet freedom is unlikely. But a carved-out research and business internet corridor with reliable access to development tools and cloud platforms would yield returns far exceeding its cost. Under Scenario B, full internet freedom becomes a day-one priority.

Stop centralizing decisions that should be local. Water management in Kerman requires different solutions than in East Azerbaijan. Energy challenges in Khuzestan at fifty-two degrees are different from Tabriz. Seismic risk in Tehran is different from Zahedan. National targets set direction. They are useless for implementation unless provinces have the authority, budget, and accountability to adapt them to local conditions. Every previous national plan treated provinces as identical subdivisions. Every previous plan produced uniform mediocrity.

V. Six Crises Already Killing People

Iran does not need to invent priorities. The priorities are screaming. Six problems damage Iranian lives now, are shared by neighboring countries (creating natural export markets), and are within reach of Iran's existing scientific base. These are not "missions" in the bureaucratic sense of a centrally managed program with a budget and reporting hierarchy. They are problems that need solving. A mission is owned by the government. A problem is owned by the people who suffer from it. The state's role: remove obstacles, set quality standards, get out of the way.

Problem 1: The Water Collapse

Iran consumed its thousand-year water reserve in three decades. Between 2003 and 2019, the country lost approximately 211 cubic kilometers of total water storage, more than twice its annual consumption (Saemian et al., Journal of Hydrology, 2022). Over three hundred of six hundred identified aquifers are in critical condition. Some five hundred thousand illegal wells extract roughly fifty billion cubic meters annually. Over forty thousand square kilometers of territory, an area larger than the Netherlands, is undergoing measurable land subsidence. Rates exceed thirty-five centimeters per year in parts of Kerman Province and thirty-one centimeters per year in parts of Tehran Province (GFZ Helmholtz Centre, 2024).

At the household level: a farmer in South Khorasan whose well ran dry, now trucking water at ten times the cost. A family in a Tehran suburb with cracked walls because the ground has sunk fifteen centimeters. A reservoir behind a dam that fills to barely forty percent of capacity. This is not an environmental concern for future generations. It is an infrastructure emergency happening now.

The response is not a grand national water plan. It is three specific interventions, each of which pays for itself.

First, fix the leaks. Urban water networks lose between twenty-five and forty percent of treated water before it reaches a tap. The technology exists and is not complicated: leak-detection sensors, pressure management, digital metering. The governance fix is equally simple: pay technology providers based on verified loss reduction, not on equipment delivery. If losses drop by the agreed percentage, the provider gets paid. If not, the provider does not. This is a performance contract. It works because the provider's money is at risk, which is the only reliable way to align incentives.

Second, measure the water. No enforcement of extraction limits is possible without measurement. Iran needs a real-time groundwater accounting system for its most stressed basins, combining satellite data (Iran already has domestic satellite capability), well-level sensors, and enforceable extraction caps. Australia's Murray-Darling Basin Authority managed a comparable crisis through transparent data, tradeable water rights, and enforceable limits. The model exists. The question is whether Iran's government will enforce limits on politically connected users who currently extract without consequence.

Third, reuse industrial water. Iran's industrial sector uses a growing share of water with almost no incentive to recycle. Reuse procurement frameworks within industrial clusters, starting with Isfahan's steel and textile corridor and Pardis's chemicals zone, with verified targets of forty percent reduction in freshwater dependence by 2032, would relieve aquifer pressure while creating a domestic water-treatment industry.

Every country between Morocco and Pakistan shares some version of this crisis. Jordan rations water. Iraq's southern marshlands are drying. The Gulf states spend billions on desalination but lack upstream management technologies. If Iran produces verified, cost-competitive water-efficiency solutions, proven in its own harshest basins, the regional market is not speculative. It is a queue.

Problem 2: The Energy Catastrophe

In summer 2024, Iran's electricity system broke down again. Peak demand exceeded supply by an estimated fourteen to twenty thousand megawatts. Factories in some provinces lost power four days a week. Government offices and banks closed. In Khuzestan, temperatures reached fifty-two degrees. Water-treatment plants lost power, turning the water crisis into a combined water-and-electricity crisis. The grid's nominal capacity exceeds ninety-three thousand megawatts, but actual output is far lower: aging thermal plants, thirteen percent transmission losses, seasonal gas-supply constraints, collapsing hydroelectric output from drought-depleted reservoirs.

For an ordinary family: a mother in Ahvaz puts her children to sleep in fifty-degree heat because the air conditioner has been off for six hours. A shopkeeper in Kerman whose refrigerated goods spoil for the third time this summer. A hospital in Bandar Abbas on emergency generators, again, mid-surgery. The inability to keep the lights on is the most basic failure of governance. The state cannot deliver the one service every other service depends on.

The interventions that matter are small, distributed, and quick.

Deploy grid-forming battery storage at the fifty nodes most vulnerable to heat-driven overload, starting with Khuzestan, Hormozgan, and southern Fars. This does not require new power plants. It requires buffering the existing system at its worst moments. Cost: a fraction of a new thermal plant. Impact: immediate.

Set minimum efficiency standards for new cooling equipment. Create financing that pays for building-level cooling upgrades from measured energy savings. Air conditioning is the single largest driver of peak demand growth. An Iranian manufacturer producing certified high-efficiency cooling units can sell domestically and to Iraq, Afghanistan, and Central Asia, all markets with the same heat problem.

Require distributed backup power (solar-plus-storage, properly sized) at all hospitals, water-treatment plants, and emergency response centers. The technical capability exists in Iran. What has been missing is a mandate with enforcement.

The entire Persian Gulf and Central Asian corridor faces the same challenge. Iraq's grid collapsed repeatedly in 2023 and 2024. Pakistan faces chronic load-shedding. Uzbekistan and Turkmenistan have aging Soviet-era infrastructure. A country with verified, cost-effective grid-stability components for hot climates will find buyers.

Problem 3: The Earthquake That Has Not Happened Yet

Iran averages over six thousand earthquakes per year. It sits on the collision zone between the Arabian and Eurasian tectonic plates. This is a geological fact as permanent as the mountains it built. No policy can change the geology. Policy can only change the consequences.

Sixty percent of buildings in Tehran, a city of nine million, do not meet anti-seismic standards. A magnitude 6.5 earthquake directly beneath the capital would seriously damage these structures, destroying roughly twenty percent entirely (Radio Farda, 2024). Hospital earthquake preparedness has been assessed as moderate at best, with structural safety rated the weakest component (Nasiripour et al., 2024).

This is the purest example of a hidden catastrophic risk: it costs nothing while it does not happen and costs everything when it does. The February 2023 Turkey-Syria earthquake killed over fifty-three thousand people in Turkey, a country with far more advanced construction regulations than Iran. Regulations without enforcement are useless. Enforcement without accountability is theater.

Three parts, and the ordering matters.

Start with schools and hospitals. Retrofit every school and hospital in the highest-hazard seismic zones within five years. Use performance contracts: engineering firms get paid when an independent structural audit confirms the retrofit meets the standard. Not before. Schools and hospitals are public buildings where the government is the buyer, meaning procurement reform can be implemented without waiting for the private construction market to change.

Deploy continuous structural monitoring on bridges, dams, pipelines, and high-rise buildings using low-cost sensors. Iran's existing capabilities in sensor design, developed partly under sanctions-driven self-sufficiency, provide a domestic technology base. Feed the data into a risk-prioritization dashboard so retrofit budgets go where data says the risk is highest, not where political connections are strongest.

Reform enforcement. Building codes exist. Compliance is inconsistent. The engineering profession (Nezam-e Mohandesi) and municipal authorities need a joint auditing mechanism that makes enforcement as routine as financial auditing. The goal is not perfection. The goal: when the earthquake comes, and it will come, the death toll is counted in hundreds, not tens of thousands.

Turkey, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Iraq, and the Caucasus countries all sit on active seismic zones. Iran's civil engineering experience positions it to provide retrofit services, monitoring systems, and resilience materials regionally. Credibility abroad starts with credibility at home.

Problem 4: The Medicine That Cannot Be Sanctioned

Pharmaceuticals may be the area where sanctions have most accidentally created domestic capability. Iran manufactures ninety-five to ninety-nine percent of medicines consumed domestically, one of the highest self-sufficiency rates in the developing world (IFDA, 2024). Biotechnology product exports exceed two hundred million dollars to thirty-five countries. Iran ranks first among Islamic nations in biotechnology research output and eleventh globally. Pharmaceutical exports grew sixteen percent in the fiscal year ending March 2025.

A genuine success built on genuine necessity. But the same sector suffers from scale limitations, uneven quality assurance, thin clinical trial governance, and limited international regulatory recognition. Illicit Iranian-made medicines have been found in both Iraq and Afghanistan. Demand exists. Quality assurance has not kept pace with production. The visible benefit: self-sufficiency. The hidden cost: a reputation problem that could destroy the export opportunity before it matures.

The interventions that matter are not about producing more medicine. They are about making it trustworthy.

Scale up bioprocessing with mandatory adherence to WHO prequalification standards, not as an aspiration but as a condition of public funding. Identify the five largest facilities, map equipment gaps and process validation weaknesses, fund targeted upgrades with the explicit requirement that WHO standards are met within three years. Facilities that miss the standard lose public funding. Managers bear the cost of failure, not just enjoy the benefits of success.

Develop affordable diagnostics for regional demand. Identify twenty diagnostic products with high domestic demand and clear export potential. Create a streamlined regulatory pathway for kits meeting international benchmarks. Establish initial supply agreements with health ministries in Iraq, Afghanistan, and Tajikistan.

Fix clinical trial credibility. Establish a unified national protocol for trial transparency, independent ethics review, and data-integrity audit. Create a publicly accessible registry of all publicly funded trials with mandatory reporting of positive and negative results, following the AllTrials initiative and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform. When a purchasing officer in Baghdad decides between an Iranian biosimilar and an Indian one, the question is: can I trust the data? Every fraudulent paper, every unreported negative result makes the answer more likely to be no.

Iran's non-oil exports to Iraq alone reached $11.2 billion in eleven months ending February 2025. A country producing quality-verified biosimilars at competitive prices occupies a market position geopolitics cannot easily dislodge, because the product is essential and the price is right. But only if the quality is real.

Problem 5: Selling Raw Materials and Buying Them Back as Products

Iran exported approximately nineteen billion dollars in petrochemical products in the fiscal year ending March 2024, the country's largest non-oil export category. The vast majority are commodity feedstocks: methanol, urea, polyethylene. These travel to foreign plants, get converted into higher-value products, then get sold back to Iran at several times the price. This is the classic resource-curse pattern. The visible benefit: export revenue. The hidden cost: permanent dependency on others to do the hard, profitable part.

The standard policy response is to announce a plan to "move up the value chain." Iran has announced several. They failed. Moving up a value chain requires specific technical capabilities, specific quality certifications, specific customer relationships, and specific financing structures. None of these can be willed into existence by a government announcement.

The interventions that would actually work are narrow and concrete.

Iran's large petrochemical plants depend heavily on imported catalysts, a dependency sanctions have made both expensive and unreliable. Create a challenge program designed jointly with the major plants in the Pars Special Economic Energy Zone. Fund domestic catalyst development linked to real procurement contracts. The buyer commits to purchase if specifications are met, verified by an independent testing laboratory. This is not a general research fund. It is a specific bet: a plant in Assaluyeh that imported catalysts from Germany at four hundred thousand dollars per batch gets the chance to source domestically at half the price with verified performance. If the catalyst works, the buyer saves money and the producer gains a customer. If it does not work, no one pretends it did.

Deploy efficiency upgrades at the twenty facilities with the largest methane emissions, using shared-savings contracts. The operator and the technology provider split the value of captured gas. No government spending needed after contracts are signed. The mechanism is self-sustaining because the waste has monetary value.

Build up domestic industrial suppliers (valves, pipe fittings, instrumentation, process equipment) through quality certification ladders tied to procurement access. Firms that invest in design capability and obtain international certifications (ISO, API, ASME) gain preferred access to public contracts. This is not a subsidy. It is a conditional reward for becoming competitive.

Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Oman, and Turkmenistan are building industrial infrastructure at enormous scale. Every project needs catalysts, specialty chemicals, engineered components, and service providers. If Iranian suppliers demonstrate quality parity through independently verified performance rather than government endorsement, they become natural commercial partners. The relationship shifts from geopolitical tension to commercial interdependence.

Problem 6: The Digital Nervous System

Digital capability is not a separate sector. It is the infrastructure connecting every other problem. A water-monitoring network without reliable data transmission produces noise. A seismic-risk dashboard without interoperable data standards produces confusion. A biotech export program without digital supply-chain management breaks at the first customs delay.

Iran's internet penetration stands at approximately ninety-four percent, but effective digital capacity is hobbled by low broadband speeds, frequent shutdowns, filtering policies, and restricted access to major global platforms. Researchers and tech firms often cannot use standard cloud services, version-control platforms, or AI training frameworks, not because of technical inability but because of sanctions and domestic censorship.

The interventions are straightforward but politically difficult under Scenario A.

Persian, with over one hundred and ten million speakers including Dari and Tajik, is underserved in the global AI landscape. Fund Persian-language AI models and domain-specific tools for water management, energy operations, industrial optimization, and health informatics. The prerequisite: a data-governance protocol defining how data is collected, stored, and shared across agencies. Without usable data, no AI system is worth building.

Fix public data infrastructure. Government data systems (civil registration, health records, industrial statistics) are fragmented, inconsistently digitized, and inaccessible across agencies. Create interoperability standards with real deadlines and real consequences.

Deploy practical digital tools to small and medium enterprises through corridor-based pilots in five provinces. A carpet workshop in Tabriz that currently sells through three middlemen should be able to track orders, manage inventory, and reach buyers in Dubai through a platform with a Persian interface and local support. Measure productivity before and after. If the tools work, expand. If not, try different tools.

Persian AI tools serve not only Iran but Afghanistan, Tajikistan, and Persian-speaking communities across Central Asia. The software and applications developed for these problems can be adapted for any neighboring market with similar challenges.

VI. How to Govern This: Small Bets, Accountability, and Consequences

The governance section of any strategy document is where ambition goes to die. Governance is where the interests of those who hold power collide with the changes the strategy demands. Iran's previous strategies handled this collision by adding new institutions on top of old ones, another council, another office, another coordination mechanism, creating new opportunities for the same people to obstruct progress through additional layers.

The governing principle here is different. Do not add new institutions. Remove unnecessary ones. Do not centralize more decisions. Push decisions to the people closest to the problem. Do not create new oversight mechanisms. Make existing ones work by attaching real consequences to failure.

Consolidate, do not add. Iran does not need another science council. Consolidate the science-governance responsibilities of the Supreme Council of Cultural Revolution, the Vice Presidency for Science and Technology, and the Ministry of Science into a single body with three powers: authority to select and terminate projects based on field data; budget authority to reallocate funds from underperformers within each fiscal year; obligation to publish an annual performance report reviewed by independent auditors. Under Scenario B, a successor government can design science governance from scratch, without the legacy of revolutionary ideology, Basij involvement in university administration, or security-state interference.

Run everything through contracts with expiration dates. Each problem area gets a dedicated delivery unit on a three-year renewable contract with annual performance gates and quarterly public reporting. Miss two consecutive gates without an approved redesign, and the budget gets reallocated automatically. The UK Prime Minister's Delivery Unit (2001-2005) and Malaysia's PEMANDU (2009-2015) demonstrated that time-limited, outcome-measured units improve execution within existing bureaucracies. The key is the time limit. A delivery unit that exists forever is just another department.

Let provinces adapt. Each problem operates through regional boards connecting provincial problem-owners (utilities, hospitals, municipalities) with industrial actors, universities, and export institutions. Regional boards translate national objectives into local plans accounting for different water tables, industrial bases, border-trade relationships, and climates.

Build a real matchmaking function between problems and solvers. A utility director in Dezful with a water-loss problem and a sensor startup in Tehran with a potential solution have no structured way to find each other. Iran has fragments of matchmaking infrastructure (the Techmart platform at Pardis Technology Park, the Center for International Science and Technology Cooperation, science park events) but they are fragmented, voluntary, and disconnected from procurement budgets.

What is needed draws on four proven international models. The US SBIR program requires federal agencies to fund competitive awards for small businesses in three phases: feasibility, development, commercialization. The business retains IP; the government gets a royalty-free license. Before funding, every proposal must answer specific questions: what are you trying to do, how is it done today, what is new, who benefits, what are the risks, what are the checkpoints? This discipline, forcing proposals through concrete questions before money moves, is exactly what Iran's grant system lacks. Chile's CORFO co-finances technology adoption by small firms. The UK's Catapult network operates on a thirds model: one-third government grants, one-third competitive research funding, one-third direct commercial contracts, ensuring each center earns commercial credibility. Oman's EJAAD platform provides a digital marketplace where companies post technical challenges with defined specifications and budgets.

Iran's function should combine all four: gather structured problem statements from utilities, hospitals, municipalities, and firms; assemble delivery teams; structure pilot contracts with milestones and payment triggers tied to performance; publish field outcomes publicly. The difference between what exists and what is needed is the difference between a bulletin board and a brokerage with accountability.

Three budget rules that make everything else honest:

One: at least thirty percent of all funding released only after verified field performance. Not after equipment delivery. Not after report submission. After independently measured outcomes. A water program releases its second payment when an independent meter confirms losses dropped by the agreed percentage. Not before.

Two: public procurement contracts become the primary market-creation instrument. When a ministry buys technology, the purchase is structured to build supplier capability. The buyer evaluates whether the technology was absorbed, whether local engineers learned, whether the supplier became more capable.

Three: any project that fails to meet agreed metrics within one year of pilot completion is redesigned under strict conditions or terminated. Resources flow to what works. This rule must be enforced without exception, especially when the failing project is connected to someone powerful. The moment it is waived for political reasons, the entire system loses credibility.

VII. The Talent Catastrophe

The numbers in this section are large enough to lose their meaning, so here they are in concrete form.

A chemistry professor in Shiraz earns approximately one thousand dollars a month. She trained for fifteen years, runs a research lab, publishes in international journals. Her graduate students leave for Canada and Germany, where stipends exceed her salary. Twenty-five percent of Iranian university faculty have left their positions in recent years, migration being a dominant factor, a figure acknowledged by Iran's own Minister of Science in 2024 (Times Higher Education, December 2024). An emergency room in Ahvaz cannot fill its specialist roster: three cardiologists emigrated the same year. A software startup in Tehran loses its lead developer to Dubai every eighteen months.

The World Bank estimates the annual economic cost of Iran's brain drain at fifty billion dollars, more than twice annual oil revenue in a typical sanctions year. Iranian students abroad: one hundred and ten thousand, nearly doubled in four years. Return rate for those who leave for advanced study: less than one percent. Healthcare professionals who left in 2023 alone: three thousand. Between 2007 and 2012, ninety-six percent of patents by Iranian-born inventors were filed from outside the country.

A country that spends two decades building university capacity and then exports a quarter of its faculty is running a knowledge-subsidy program for the rest of the world: paying the cost of training, donating the returns. The visible benefit of cheap academic labor is a large university system. The hidden cost is the hollowing out of every field that matters.

The response has four parts, and none involve asking people to stay out of loyalty.

Pay people properly. A full professor in Iran: one thousand dollars per month. Same qualification in a Gulf state: four to seven thousand. Faculty who lead work with demonstrable field outcomes should earn salaries competitive with the regional median. A professor who leaves costs the system far more than a raise would have. This is arithmetic, not generosity.

Create return pathways with real substance. For diaspora professionals: joint appointments, visiting fellowships, industry-embedded positions tied to specific deliverables. The Indian Institutes of Technology built a pipeline of returning engineers through industry-linked positions and competitive research autonomy. An Iranian-born materials scientist at MIT who could lead a catalysts challenge program needs a credible professional offer, not a guilt trip. Under Scenario B, the pathways become dramatically more attractive: property-rights protection, independent courts, academic freedom, personal safety, absence of security-state interference. Germany's post-war experience is instructive. Emigrant scientists returned once institutions were trustworthy. Not before.

Build network roles for those who will not return. Create formal roles as advisors, quality auditors, international market connectors, and mentors. Israel's BIRD Foundation shows how bilateral technology-development vehicles keep diaspora talent engaged without requiring relocation.

Redesign graduate training around real problems. Every publicly funded doctoral and master's program in relevant fields should require a minimum of six months in an industrial or field-deployment setting. The German Fraunhofer model, where applied-research institutes sit at the university-industry interface with researchers moving between academic and industrial roles, provides a tested framework. A student who spends six months at a water utility comes back with a problem worth solving. A student who never leaves the lab solves problems that may not exist outside it.

Under Scenario B, the talent equation transforms more than any other dimension. The Iran Reconstruction Conference in Silicon Valley (February 2025) brought together diaspora professionals with specific proposals for technology transfer, institutional reform, and capital mobilization (Iranaissance Foundation, 2025). The Iran Prosperity Project (July 2025) proposed emergency stabilization followed by institutional reform. The combined assets of three to five million diaspora Iranians likely exceed one trillion dollars. Iranian-American executives, including technology company leaders, have publicly discussed contributing to a transitional Iran's development (Washington Post, 2025). But they will invest only if property rights are protected, courts are independent, and the regulatory environment is predictable. These are governance preconditions, not financial ones.

VIII. Research Integrity: The Foundation Nobody Wants to Talk About

A strategy depending on verified performance, quality-assured exports, and trusted clinical data cannot afford to have its scientific output questioned. Iran has a research-integrity problem. It is not unique (every high-volume publication system has one), but in the context of an export strategy it is not a footnote. It is a deal-breaker.

Four parts.

Prevention: every publicly funded researcher undergoes mandatory training in reproducibility standards, data management, and publication ethics. The training includes assessed competencies, not attendance certificates.

Transparency: all publicly funded research publishes data, methods, and negative results in open-access repositories. Selective reporting (publishing only positive outcomes) is treated as a violation of contract terms.

Audit: an independent National Research Integrity Office, structurally separated from the institutions it oversees, conducts random and targeted audits. Published annually.

Enforcement: verified misconduct results in funding exclusion, public disclosure, and institutional penalties. The sanctions must be credible to be preventive. If misconduct is discovered and nothing happens, the audit system is worse than useless: it creates the illusion of oversight without the reality.

This is not a moral crusade. It is an economic calculation. A hospital purchasing officer in Baghdad who discovers that clinical trial data behind an Iranian biosimilar was manipulated will not buy from Iran again. And will tell every other purchasing officer. Trust takes years to build and seconds to destroy. Iran's integrity system is either a competitive advantage or a competitive liability. There is nothing in between.

IX. Financing: Honest Numbers for Dishonest Times

Any financing plan must reckon with early 2026 reality: GDP contracting by an estimated 2.8 percent, inflation between forty-two and sixty percent, currency in freefall, public revenues under severe pressure. Grand spending targets, the kind previous plans loved, are dishonest under these conditions.

Under Scenario A: stabilize R&D spending at approximately 0.7 to 0.8 percent of a shrinking GDP through 2028, then gradually increase to one percent by 2030. Prioritize programs that generate immediate returns. Water-loss recovery saves money from day one. Energy-efficiency retrofits reduce import dependency. Pharmaceutical quality upgrades open export markets. Programs requiring large capital expenditure without near-term returns get phased later. This is not retreat. This is triage.

Under Scenario B: target 1.5 percent by 2032, two percent by 2035, 2.5 percent by 2040, with the private-sector share rising from roughly twenty percent to at least fifty percent. Turkey's R&D spending rose from 0.5 to 1.1 percent in a decade. These numbers are achievable if the system earns private-sector confidence through transparency and demonstrated returns.

Three financing mechanisms work under Scenario A.

For programs with quantifiable returns (water-loss recovery, energy efficiency, methane capture), blended-finance instruments attract institutional investors by combining public first-loss capital with private return-seeking capital. Iran's capital market, despite limitations, has enough depth for small-denomination bonds if projects are transparent and performance data independently verified.

A structured diaspora investment channel, modeled on India's NRI bond program, offers Iranians abroad the opportunity to invest in specific projects with published metrics and independent oversight. The mechanism treats diaspora members as investors, not cultural symbols.

A fixed percentage of oil revenues above a baseline price (the windfall portion, not base revenue) legally earmarked for technology-infrastructure investment, with disbursement tied to performance dashboards. This links resource wealth to technology transition rather than letting windfalls be consumed by current spending.

Under Scenario B, four additional mechanisms become available: international reconstruction financing with institutional reform conditions; World Bank and IMF program lending; foreign direct investment; and bilateral technology-cooperation agreements with the EU, Japan, South Korea, and India.

X. The Elephant: The IRGC's Economic Empire

This strategy has been written as if the main obstacles are technical and institutional. That framing is deliberately incomplete. The single largest obstacle to everything proposed here is the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps.

The IRGC is not merely a military organization. Through Khatam al-Anbiya Construction Headquarters alone, it controls the country's largest engineering and contracting operation, holding no-bid contracts across oil, gas, transportation, telecommunications, mining, and water infrastructure. Estimates of the IRGC's share of economic activity range from twenty to fifty percent (CSIS, 2023; TRT World, 2024; Council on Foreign Relations, 2024). IRGC-affiliated entities have expanded systematically into sectors vacated by foreign companies after each sanctions round, meaning sanctions designed to pressure the regime have enlarged the Guards' economic footprint. The visible benefit of sanctions: pressure on the regime. The hidden cost: a military-economic complex that grows stronger with each restriction.

The implications are direct. A matchmaking function that must award contracts on merit collides with a procurement system giving IRGC-affiliated firms preferential access. A delivery unit that must terminate underperformers faces political resistance when the underperformer is connected to the Guards. A regional board coordinating provincial actors will find that in many provinces the most powerful economic actor is already the IRGC. University research priorities, faculty appointments, and student admissions are shaped by security considerations unrelated to scientific merit. The brain drain is not only economic; for many who left, it is a direct consequence of the security state.

Under Scenario A, the strategy works around the IRGC rather than through it. Programs with clear economic returns and low political sensitivity (water-loss recovery, pharmaceutical quality, seismic retrofit of schools) can advance because they align with the regime's survival interest in maintaining basic services. But the governance reforms at this document's heart (transparent procurement, independent auditing, merit-based allocation, authority to terminate politically connected projects) strike directly at the economic model sustaining IRGC power. There is no evidence the Guards would accept a system where their affiliates compete on equal terms.

Under Scenario B, unwinding the IRGC's economic empire becomes the hardest practical problem a successor government will face. Iran's own privatization history is cautionary: past transfers of state assets overwhelmingly benefited regime insiders, with only twenty to thirty percent reaching genuine private ownership (Iran Wire, 2024).

A serious dismantlement would proceed in five steps. First: forensic audit of all IRGC-affiliated entities (assets, revenues, personnel, contracts). You cannot reform what you have not mapped. Second: legally separate legitimate infrastructure operations from rent-seeking enterprises and military funding streams. Third: break monopolistic holdings into smaller units and open them to competitive bidding through transparent, internationally supervised auctions, following Czech and Polish post-Soviet models rather than Russia's oligarch-capturing approach. Fourth: protect workers. The IRGC employs hundreds of thousands of ordinary Iranians in construction, logistics, and services. Dismantlement without job absorption produces mass unemployment. Germany's Treuhandanstalt provides both positive lessons (rapid transformation) and cautionary ones (regional devastation). Fifth: convert frozen IRGC assets into public trust instruments whose returns fund civilian technology infrastructure.

Under Scenario A, this framework is academic. Under Scenario B, it is the most immediately consequential policy challenge in the country.

XI. International Reintegration: A Readiness Plan

This section is relevant only under Scenario B, but the day a transition begins is not the day to start planning reintegration. It is the day the plan should already exist.

Sanctions relief would proceed in stages, based on precedents from Iraq, Myanmar, Sudan, and the 2015 JCPOA: humanitarian and banking access first (reconnection to SWIFT, correspondent banking, essential-goods trade); oil-export normalization with phased increases and monitored accounts; technology access (removal of dual-use restrictions, legal access to cloud computing, developer tools, AI training frameworks); full trade normalization conditional on governance reform and IAEA compliance.

Scientific reintegration would reopen CERN participation, expanded WHO collaboration on pharmaceutical regulation, IAEA safeguards modernization, and EU Horizon research programs. WTO accession (Iran has held observer status since 2005) would require trade liberalization, intellectual property reform, and transparent regulation.

Bilateral technology agreements, structured as joint-funded programs with defined deliverables rather than vague memoranda of understanding, would provide technology access and market entry. Priority partnerships: EU for water technology and renewable energy; Japan for seismic engineering and industrial efficiency; South Korea for digital infrastructure and battery technology; India for pharmaceutical manufacturing and agricultural technology; Gulf states for industrial services and petrochemical technology.

Academic freedom must become a measurable benchmark. A post-transition Iran must end security-state interference in universities: Basij organizations monitoring faculty, intelligence screenings shaping admissions, mandatory ideological coursework. Academic freedom is not a luxury. It is a precondition for the research culture this strategy requires.

XII. Timeline: What Has to Happen First

2026-2028: Prove that accountability is real. The first eighteen months determine everything. The test is not whether grand institutions are created but whether small things work. Does the first water-loss pilot actually reduce losses, verified by an independent meter? Does the first seismic retrofit pass an independent structural audit? Does the first pharmaceutical facility achieve WHO prequalification? Does the first underperforming project actually get terminated? If these happen, the system is real. If not, everything in this document is fiction.

Specific actions: formalize legal authority for the consolidated STI body; appoint delivery units under published contracts with defined stage gates; launch the matchmaking function with structured problem statements from utilities, hospitals, and industrial firms; open regulatory test environments in water technology, energy systems, and health biotech; issue procurement guidelines; publish a public performance dashboard with quarterly updates; require provinces to publish corridor-level implementation plans; make integrity requirements mandatory for all publicly funded research from day one.

2029-2033: Scale what works, kill what does not. Validated pilots become corridor-scale programs. Standards and certification systems are strengthened. Procurement volumes increase for proven domestic solutions. Export-preparation pipelines become defined, resourced processes. By the end of this phase, an Iraqi water utility should be able to evaluate an Iranian system with the same confidence it evaluates a European one, because the performance data is public and the track record is verified.

2034-2040: From products to systems. Iran transitions from exporting products to exporting integrated systems and long-cycle services: engineering, operations, maintenance, digital support packages. The test: when Uzbekistan's water authority issues a modernization tender, an Iranian consortium bids competitively, not because of diplomatic pressure but because its track record is publicly documented and independently verified.

XIII. What Success Actually Looks Like

By 2040, success should not be measured by publications, parks, or registered companies. Those are inputs. Success is measured by outputs people can feel.

A family in South Khorasan turns on the tap and the water flows. Not trucked, not rationed. Just there. The electricity holds through a July afternoon in Ahvaz. A child in Bam walks into a school her parents know will stand because an independent engineer certified it. A patient in Zahedan receives medication her doctor trusts because the clinical data is public and the facility independently audited. A young engineer in Tabriz stays because the salary is competitive, the work is serious, and the outcome matters.

Measurable benchmarks: private-sector share of R&D spending above forty-five percent; at least sixty percent of budgets governed by milestone contracts; knowledge-intensive non-oil exports growing at twelve percent annually from 2028; faculty attrition reduced by at least fifty percent from 2024 levels; retraction rates below the global average; at least five operational multi-year supply contracts with regional buyers in each problem area; net talent flow in critical fields turning positive by 2035.

The truest test is simpler. It is whether Iran has become a country that its own people trust to deliver basic services, and that its neighbors trust enough to buy from. The countries the world trades with most willingly are the countries that make things that work. Not the countries that published the most papers. Not the countries that announced the most ambitious plans. The countries that made things work, proved it, and kept doing it long enough for the proof to matter.

Iran's previous strategies were ambitious, comprehensive, and named the right goals. They failed because they assumed a detailed plan could substitute for the messy, local, unpredictable work of actually solving problems, and because the political system rewarded the appearance of progress over the fact of it.

This approach is different. Specific problems already killing people. Small interventions that pay for themselves. Accountability through money tied to measured results. Reforms that are removals: of obstacles, of failing projects, of bureaucratic complexity, of political interference. Works under the current regime. Works better without it. Treats the diaspora as investors, not nostalgic symbols. Measures success by what comes out of the tap, not what goes into the report.

The architecture exists. The problems are defined. The evidence is assembled. The rest is a question of who governs, and for whom.

Appendix A: Weakest Assumptions Exposed

  1. "Performance contracts will be enforced against politically connected entities." Under Scenario A, the IRGC controls twenty to fifty percent of the economy. The assumption that a delivery unit can terminate an underperforming IRGC-affiliated project is the strategy's most fragile claim. Without this enforcement, the entire accountability architecture is decorative.

  2. "The diaspora will invest based on published metrics and independent oversight." Diaspora capital requires property-rights protection, independent courts, and regulatory predictability. Under Scenario A, none of these exist. The diaspora investment channel is essentially a Scenario B instrument dressed up as dual-track.

  3. "Iran can earn regional export trust through independently verified quality." This assumes credible independent verification bodies exist or can be created. Under sanctions, international auditing firms cannot operate freely in Iran. Domestic bodies face the same political pressures that corrupt everything else. Who audits the auditor?

  4. "Water extraction limits can be enforced on politically connected users." Five hundred thousand illegal wells exist because enforcement has been politically impossible for decades. The strategy proposes transparent measurement as a prerequisite for enforcement, but transparency without political will to act on it is just a more detailed picture of the problem you are choosing not to solve.

  5. "Thirty percent milestone-linked funding will survive budget politics." When fiscal pressure intensifies (and at 42-60 percent inflation, it will), the temptation to release milestone funds early "to keep projects alive" will be enormous. The rule either survives its first political test or becomes one more Iranian regulation that exists on paper.

  6. "R&D spending targets are meaningful during economic collapse." Targeting 0.7-0.8 percent of a shrinking GDP means the absolute amount spent on research is falling even as the percentage holds. The percentage target creates a false sense of stability. The real question is whether the absolute amount is enough to run even the proposed pilots.

  7. "Province-level adaptation will produce innovation rather than province-level capture." Pushing decisions local is correct in principle, but in Iran's context, provincial power structures are often more corrupt and more captured by local IRGC-affiliated interests than national ones. Decentralization without local accountability just moves the bottleneck.

Appendix B: Five Sharper Lines

  1. Iran does not have an innovation problem. It has a deployment problem. The laboratories are full. The taps are dry.

  2. The country that built drones to kill its neighbors' citizens and facial recognition to control its own has demonstrated, conclusively, that its technical talent is real and its moral compass is broken.

  3. A strategy that cannot survive the question "what happens when the IRGC affiliate gets the contract?" is not a strategy. It is a wish list with footnotes.

  4. Iran's greatest export is not petrochemicals. It is trained scientists. The difference is that petrochemicals leave by pipeline and scientists leave by choice.

  5. Every Iranian science plan since 2005 has had the same structure: correct diagnosis, ambitious targets, zero consequences for failure. This document's only innovation is insisting that consequences are not optional.
