Extra Branches

A play, second version

1404/12/19

Characters

SON — a man in his mid-forties.

FATHER — a man in his mid-seventies. Thirty years a civil servant at the Agricultural Crusade. Thin. His hands are strong.

MOTHER — a woman in her mid-sixties.

NILOOFAR — a woman the son's age.

ZARI — a woman the son's age. She wears a chador.

Setting

The stage is almost empty. At its center: a half-built cage. Wire, board, tools.

All the characters are present onstage from the beginning. Each in a different spot, in a different time, motionless. When the light falls on them, they come alive. When the light leaves, they freeze. No one ever exits the stage.

The cage grows over the course of the play. Each scene adds a piece to it. By the end of the play it is almost complete.

(Darkness. The sound of wire bending. The sound of breath. Slowly the light rises: yellow, slanted, evening. The Father beside the cage. A tool in his hand. The Son stands, watching from a distance.)

(At the margins of the stage: the Mother sits on a chair, motionless. Niloofar stands, facing the wall. Zari has wrapped her chador around herself, her head down. None of them move. Not yet.)

FATHER: Come hold this side.

(The Son goes. Kneels beside the Father. Holds the wire. The Father bends it. The sound of wire.)

(Work. Breath. Silence.)

SON: Is it solid?

FATHER: It will be solid.

(The work continues. The Father cuts a wire. The Son holds.)

(Suddenly: a phone ringing. From somewhere. The Son turns his head. The Father does not raise his.)

(The ringing continues.)

FATHER: (without looking) Leave it.

(The ringing does not stop. The Son rises. Goes toward the sound. The phone is on the ground, center stage.)

(The Son picks up the phone.)

FATHER'S VOICE: (from the phone, warm, ordinary) Hello, son. Are you well?

(The Son looks at the Father, who is still sitting beside the cage, wire in hand. The Father on the phone and the Father building the cage are present at the same time.)

SON: (into the phone) I'm well.

FATHER'S VOICE: Your mother sends her greetings. She says, why don't you call.

SON: I call.

(The Father beside the cage bends a wire. The sound of wire.)

FATHER'S VOICE: Well. Mind yourself. Don't forget to say your prayers.

(Silence. From somewhere in the dark: the sound of laughter. The laughter of friends. Soft at first, then louder. The Son moves the phone away from his ear.)

(The laughter continues. The Son sets the phone on the ground. The laughter stops.)

(The Father beside the cage:)

FATHER: Hand me the wire.

(The Son returns. Hands him the wire. Work again. Silence again.)

(The Mother, still on the chair, stirs. Only her hand. As if smoothing something flat.)

MOTHER'S VOICE: (not loud, from a distance) Write down your father's occupation: Crusader.

(The Son's hand stops for a moment. Then continues. As if he did not hear. Or has grown used to it.)

(The light changes. Bluer. Colder. The Son rises from beside the cage. But the Father stays where he is, still working.)

(At the margin of the stage: Zari raises her head. A little light falls on her. And farther off: Niloofar, still standing facing the wall, but now breathing.)

(The Son stands center stage. Between Zari and Niloofar. Between the cage and nothing.)

ZARI: (without looking at the Son, as if she has been in the middle of a sentence for a long time) ... Tomorrow night let's go to my mother's for dinner.

SON: (his gaze is elsewhere) All right.

ZARI: I said let's go.

SON: I heard.

(A pause. Zari arranges her chador. The motion of her hands is precise, like a thirty-year habit.)

ZARI: What are you looking at?

(The Son does not answer. Because the answer, because...)

(Niloofar, from the other side of the stage, slowly turns her profile toward the audience. Her movement is calm. A single breath.)

(The Son's gaze falls on Niloofar. Only for a moment. Then he turns it away.)

ZARI: (more quietly) Nothing.

(A long pause.)

MOTHER'S VOICE: (from the same distance, from the same chair) She's a good girl. Her family is good. She suits us.

(The Son closes his eyes. Only for a moment.)

(The Father, from beside the cage, without raising his head:)

FATHER: These branches have to be cut. A tree grows better when it's made lighter.

(The sound of pruning shears.)

ZARI: (to the Son) Are you cold?

SON: No.

ZARI: Your hands are cold.

(A pause.)

SON: (quietly, not to Zari, not to anyone) I've been cold for years.

(Zari does not hear this. Or she hears it and says nothing. Either is worse.)

(The light changes. Night. Faint yellow. The Son is lying on a mattress. The sound of music from afar: a wedding.)

(The sound of a woman's laughter from one side. The sound of men talking from the other.)

(Suddenly: the Father's voice. Loud. Angry. The words are not clear.)

(The voice of another man, Uncle Karim:)

UNCLE KARIM'S VOICE: Haj-Agha. Haj-Agha, calm down. It's a wedding.

(The Father's voice grows louder. Then it is smothered. As if someone has put a hand over his mouth. Or as if he has fallen silent himself.)

(The Son on the mattress, eyes closed.)

(From the other side of the stage: Zari. A little light on her. Sitting on another mattress. Another time.)

ZARI: (quietly) Are you asleep?

(The Son does not answer. Not to the Zari of now. Not to the wedding of then.)

ZARI: (more quietly) I know you're awake.

(A long pause.)

(The sound of the wedding again, fainter. Like a sound coming from underwater.)

ZARI: (her voice trembles a little, not from grief, from exhaustion) You were never here. Even when you were beside me.

(The Son does not move.)

(The Father, from beside the cage, as if he is still there:)

FATHER: (his voice from very far) The extra ones. The ones that waste the strength.

(The sound of pruning shears.)

(Zari wraps her chador tighter around herself. The light on her dims.)

(The light changes. Whiter. More open. The air has changed.)

(The Son rises. Slowly. His breathing has changed, as if he is breathing different air.)

(Niloofar, for the first time, turns away from the wall. Completely. She is present.)

(Two people facing each other. A distance between them.)

(A long pause. Not a word. Not a movement. Only a look.)

NILOOFAR: (simply) You're alone too.

SON: You too?

NILOOFAR: (she does not say yes. She does not say no. Only:) You can tell from the voice.

(A pause.)

SON: It's a strange thing. I don't know how to talk.

NILOOFAR: You don't have to talk.

(A pause. The Son takes a step closer. Niloofar does not step back.)

SON: I have a wife.

(A pause.)

NILOOFAR: I know.

(A long pause.)

SON: (his voice breaks, not loud) I don't know how... I mean...

(He does not finish.)

(From the other side of the stage: Zari. Her light is low but present. Her voice like a memory:)

ZARI: (motionless, from a distance) Are you asleep?

(The Son turns his head, between Zari and Niloofar. His body is stretched between two times.)

(Niloofar does not move. She is not waiting. She simply is.)

SON: (to Niloofar, quietly) I don't know how to be outside.

NILOOFAR: No one knows how.

(A pause.)

(The Father, from beside the cage, without raising his head:)

FATHER: Come hold this side.

(The Son does not go. For the first time.)

(The light does not change. The same white. But now the Mother has risen from the chair. She is standing.)

(The Mother and Father enter Niloofar's light from two sides. Slowly. Like shadows that have stood up.)

(Niloofar stands. She does not move. The Son stands between them.)

(Silence. Five people. Silence.)

(The Mother looks at Niloofar. Niloofar endures the Mother's look.)

MOTHER: (to the Son, not to Niloofar, never directly to Niloofar) Your house is cold.

SON: There's a heater.

MOTHER: Her food smells.

NILOOFAR: (quietly) I didn't have Iranian saffron. I'm sorry.

(The Mother as if she did not hear. She does not look at Niloofar.)

MOTHER: (to the Son) I brought tea. From Iran.

NILOOFAR: I have tea.

MOTHER: (to the Son) Iranian.

(A pause. Niloofar does not turn her head away. Her presence is the answer. Not resistance, not surrender.)

SON: Mom...

MOTHER: (cutting in, not angry, certain) What will people say?

SON: Which people?

MOTHER: Everyone. Everyone says it.

(A pause.)

SON: (his voice tight) I don't live for people.

(The Mother falls silent. Not from being convinced. From what she cannot hear.)

(The Father has been silent all this time. His hands are down. He looks at some point between the floor and the ceiling. He says nothing. He does not move. And this not-being, this absent presence, is the heaviest thing onstage.)

(Niloofar takes a step back. Just one step. Not flight. Making room.)

SON: (to the Father, who still does not look) Dad. Say something.

(The Father stays silent.)

SON: Dad.

(The Father raises his hand, slowly, and bends an imaginary wire. As if he is still sitting beside the cage. As if answering is only possible through building.)

(The light changes. Yellow again. Evening. The cage has grown larger. When? No one saw it. But it has more wires.)

(The Son and Father beside the cage again. Like the beginning. But now something between them has changed that neither the Son nor the Father can name.)

SON: Dad.

FATHER: Hm.

(A pause.)

SON: That friend of yours. Martyr Kazemi.

(The Father's hands slowly stop.)

SON: Why did you never go to his memorial?

(A long pause.)

FATHER: (quietly) I don't know.

SON: You don't know?

(The Father sets the wire down.)

FATHER: (looking at his hands) There was a question.

SON: What question?

(A pause.)

FATHER: Why they had to send him to the front.

(A pause.)

FATHER: (his voice changes, not louder, lower) The shrapnel, direct. His wife and child...

(He does not say it. His hands begin to tremble.)

(A pause.)

SON: (quietly, like someone asking out of fear) On Majnoon Island...

FATHER: (without preamble, like a tap turned open) The Damavand boys had come. Young. A loader driver. They went to climb on it, to build a trench.

(A pause.)

A mortar, direct.

(The hands tremble. The Son sees it.)

FATHER: (his voice breaks, only breaks) I went to gather their pieces...

(He does not finish.)

(Silence.)

(The Son reaches out his hand. Takes the wire from the Father's hand. Not with words. Just the hand. The Father allows it. The pliers open.)

(From somewhere: the Mother's voice. Quiet. As if under her breath:)

MOTHER'S VOICE: Say your prayers.

(The Father, like a reflex, his lips move. Soundless. Prayer? Or someone's name? Or only the habit of the lips when the hands cannot work?)

(The light begins to flicker. Faint. Like power cutting in and out.)

(All the characters become active, at the same time. The Mother standing. Zari sitting on the mattress. Niloofar standing. The Father beside the cage. The Son in the middle of them all. Between them all.)

(The voices begin, not together, but layered over one another:)

MOTHER'S VOICE: Write down your father's occupation...

FATHER: Come hold this side...

ZARI: Are you asleep?

FATHER'S VOICE: (on the phone) Your prayers...

NILOOFAR: You're here.

(The Son center stage. His head in his hands. The voices continue, now broken, layered, mixed:)

MOTHER: What will peo...

FATHER: The mortar dir...

ZARI: You were never he...

FATHER: The extra ones have to be...

MOTHER: Crusader...

NILOOFAR: (quieter than the rest, but clear) No one knows how.

(Suddenly: silence. Like a power cut.)

(Darkness.)

(Only the sound of breath. The Son's breath. Fast. Sweating.)

(Light, faint, on the mattress. The Son has woken from sleep. Half-risen. Panting.)

(Silence.)

SON: (a whisper) Zari?

(Silence.)

(Breath. Slower. Slower.)

(He puts his hand on his chest. His heart.)

(From the other side of the stage: Niloofar. Faint light on her. Sitting. Awake.)

NILOOFAR: (quietly) You're here.

SON: (still panting) I'm here.

(A pause.)

NILOOFAR: You were dreaming.

(The Son lies back down. Looks at the ceiling.)

SON: (quietly) I don't know if I came outside or not.

NILOOFAR: You came outside.

(A long pause.)

SON: My body. My body still...

(He does not finish.)

NILOOFAR: (she does not move) I know.

(Silence. Two people. One lying, one sitting. A distance between them. But a safe distance.)

(From afar, very quietly: the sound of wire bending.)

(The light rises. Evening. Yellow. The cage center stage, now almost complete. When was all this built?)

(The Father beside it. Like the beginning. A tool in his hand.)

(The Son rises. Slowly. He goes from the mattress toward the cage. Like the beginning. But slower. Heavier. He kneels beside the Father.)

(All the other characters are still onstage. Motionless. Shadows that have fallen asleep.)

FATHER: Come hold this side.

(The Son holds.)

(Work. Silence. Breath.)

(The Father raises his head. Looks at the Son. Directly.)

(A long pause. The two of them look. For the first time.)

FATHER: (simply) It's come out well.

(The Son looks at the cage. Then at the Father.)

SON: (a pause, then:) Dad.

FATHER: Hm.

SON: The cage. Isn't it big?

(The Father looks at the cage. His hands are calm. They do not tremble. Now they do not tremble.)

FATHER: (quietly) The birds want room.

(A pause.)

SON: (more quietly) Yes.

(Silence.)

(The light slowly dims. All the characters fade into shadow. The last thing seen: the cage.)

(The sound of birds.)

(Darkness.)
